What is software architecture in this book's framing?	The combination of a system's structure, architecture characteristics, logical components, architecture style, and architecture decisions.
What are architecture characteristics in software architecture?	The system capabilities and quality attributes, often expressed as the required "-ilities" and success criteria.
What do logical components define in software architecture?	The application's behavior, such as its domains, entities, and workflows.
What is an architecture style in software architecture?	A named structural starting point used to implement a system once its requirements and behavior are understood.
What are architecture decisions in software architecture?	Rules and constraints that guide how the system may be constructed.
Why are architecture decisions important?	They constrain implementation choices so teams build the system in ways that preserve its intended characteristics.
What is the First Law of Software Architecture?	Everything in software architecture is a trade-off.
What is the Second Law of Software Architecture?	Why is more important than how.
Why is "why" more important than "how" in software architecture?	Understanding the reasons and trade-offs behind a decision is more valuable than merely knowing the resulting structure.
What is the Third Law of Software Architecture?	Most architecture decisions are not binary but exist on a spectrum between extremes.
Why does software architecture have to be understood in context?	Architectural choices depend on the realities of the environment, including costs, constraints, and engineering practices of the time.
What is the difference between an architecture decision and a technical decision?	An architecture decision sets guiding constraints for teams, while a technical decision chooses a specific tool or implementation within those constraints.
When might a software architect need to make a specific technology choice instead of only setting guidance?	When preserving a critical architecture characteristic such as scalability, performance, or availability requires it.
Why must architects continually analyze an existing architecture?	Because changes in business needs and technology can cause structural decay and make a once-sound architecture less effective over time.
Why is keeping current with technology and industry trends important for software architects?	Architectural decisions are long-lived, so architects need awareness of trends that may affect their relevance and future fitness.
Why must architects ensure compliance with architecture decisions?	Because if teams ignore those decisions, the system can drift and fail to deliver the required characteristics.
Why should a software architect understand diverse technologies even without being an expert in all of them?	Architects need broad technical awareness to choose and integrate appropriate options in heterogeneous environments.
Why is technical breadth especially valuable for software architects?	It helps them compare options, understand trade-offs, and guide teams without being limited to one familiar tool.
Why must a software architect understand the business domain?	Because effective architecture depends on understanding the business problems, goals, and requirements the system must support.
Why are interpersonal skills essential for software architects?	Architects must lead, mentor, facilitate, and communicate decisions effectively across teams and stakeholders.
Why do software architects need negotiation and political awareness?	Architectural decisions often affect many stakeholders and are frequently challenged, so architects must justify and win support for them.
What is architectural thinking?	Viewing systems from an architectural point of view by considering structure, interactions, trade-offs, and long-term consequences.
What is the difference between software architecture and software design?	Architecture is primarily about system structure, while design is primarily about the appearance and localized implementation details within that structure.
What three criteria help place a decision on the spectrum between architecture and design?	Whether it is strategic or tactical, how much effort it takes to change, and how significant the trade-offs are.
Why are strategic decisions usually more architectural than tactical decisions?	Because they are longer-term, involve more planning and stakeholders, and shape the system more broadly.
Why does high change effort usually indicate an architectural decision?	Because architecture is generally the part of the system that is hardest to change.
Why do significant trade-offs usually indicate an architectural decision?	Because architectural choices affect important qualities and constraints across the system rather than only local implementation details.
What is technical depth?	Deep expertise in a particular language, framework, platform, or tool.
What is technical breadth?	Awareness of many different technologies, tools, and approaches, even without deep expertise in all of them.
Why is technical breadth more important than technical depth for an architect?	Architects need to compare many possible solutions and choose among them, not just optimize within one familiar technology.
What are the three levels in the knowledge pyramid for technologists?	Stuff you know; stuff you know you don't know; and stuff you don't know you don't know.
What is the career goal suggested by the knowledge pyramid?	Move topics from "don't know you don't know" into "know you don't know," and deepen only the few that are worth expert-level investment.
What common failure happens when architects try to maintain deep expertise in too many areas?	They spread themselves too thin and end up stale or ineffective across all of them.
What is stale expertise in an architect?	Outdated knowledge that the architect still treats as current and authoritative.
What is the Frozen Caveman antipattern in architecture?	Overreacting to an old failure or pet risk and letting that outdated fear dominate new architecture decisions.
What is the 20-minute rule for architects?	Spend at least 20 minutes each day learning something new or broadening technical knowledge.
Why is the 20-minute rule aimed more at breadth than depth?	Because its purpose is to keep expanding awareness of possible solutions, not to build mastery in only one area.
What is a personal technology radar?	A structured way to track technologies and techniques by how strongly you should avoid, assess, try, or adopt them.
What are the four quadrants of the Thoughtworks Technology Radar?	Tools; languages and frameworks; techniques; and platforms.
What are the four rings of the Thoughtworks Technology Radar?	Hold; assess; trial; and adopt.
What does the Hold ring mean on a technology radar?	Do not start new work with that technology, or deliberately avoid that habit or tool.
What does the Assess ring mean on a technology radar?	The technology is worth investigating further but is not yet ready for active use.
What does the Trial ring mean on a technology radar?	The technology is worth piloting on low-risk work to understand it in practice.
What does the Adopt ring mean on a technology radar?	The technology or practice is one you actively endorse and are ready to use.
Why is building a personal radar useful even if the specific entries change over time?	The exercise forces deliberate thinking about your technology portfolio instead of drifting with hype or habit.
Why should an architect treat a technology portfolio like a financial portfolio?	Because deliberate diversification reduces career risk and balances mainstream demand against exploratory bets.
Why can't architecture decisions usually be answered by search engines or an LLM alone?	Because the right choice depends on context-specific trade-offs such as constraints, risks, people, and business drivers.
In the auction example, what is the main architectural advantage of using a topic over queues?	Better extensibility and looser producer coupling, because new consumers can subscribe without changing the producer.
In the auction example, what is a major trade-off of using a topic instead of queues?	Topics make data access broader and can weaken security and control over who consumes the messages.
In the auction example, what contract trade-off exists between topics and queues?	Topics favor one shared homogeneous contract, while queues allow consumer-specific heterogeneous contracts.
In the auction example, what operational advantage can queues have over topics?	Queues can support per-consumer monitoring and autoscaling more directly.
What does it mean to translate business drivers into architecture?	Convert business goals and constraints into concrete architectural concerns such as scalability, performance, and availability.
What is the Bottleneck Trap for architects?	When an architect owns critical-path code and becomes the team's delivery bottleneck because they cannot code full-time.
How can an architect avoid the Bottleneck Trap while still coding?	Let the team own the critical path and instead code lower-risk business functionality or supporting work.
Why can proofs of concept help an architect stay hands-on?	They require real code, expose implementation details, and help validate architectural decisions.
Why should an architect write production-quality proof-of-concept code when possible?	Because POCs often become reference code, and writing them well reinforces good engineering habits.
What kinds of work can help an architect stay hands-on without blocking the team?	Proofs of concept, technical debt reduction, bug fixes, automation, architectural fitness functions, and code reviews.
What is modularity in software architecture?	The logical grouping of related code into cohesive units such as packages, namespaces, classes, functions, or components.
Why is modularity considered an implicit architectural characteristic?	Sustainable systems need modular distinction and orderly communication even when requirements never state that explicitly.
What is the difference between modularity and granularity?	Modularity is about breaking a system into parts; granularity is about how large or small those parts are.
Why can poor granularity create architecture antipatterns?	Badly sized services or components can become tightly coupled, leading to brittle distributed designs such as distributed monoliths.
Why should architects care about how developers package code?	Packaging affects reuse, coupling, analysis, and how hard the system is to restructure later.
Why is modularity useful even when physical separation has not happened yet?	Logical separation affects coupling and future restructuring even inside one monolith or codebase.
What is cohesion?	The degree to which the parts of a module belong together.
What is functional cohesion?	A module where all parts contribute directly to one well-defined responsibility.
What is sequential cohesion?	A relationship where one part's output becomes another part's input in sequence.
What is communicational cohesion?	A grouping where parts operate on the same data or contribute to the same output.
What is procedural cohesion?	A grouping where parts mainly belong together because they must run in a specific order.
What is temporal cohesion?	A grouping based mainly on things that must happen at the same time, such as startup tasks.
What is logical cohesion?	A grouping of operations that are conceptually related but not strongly functionally related.
What is coincidental cohesion?	A module whose contents are unrelated except for being placed together.
What does a high LCOM score indicate?	A lack of cohesion in methods, suggesting a class may really contain multiple unrelated responsibilities.
What is afferent coupling?	The number of incoming dependencies on a code artifact.
What is efferent coupling?	The number of outgoing dependencies from a code artifact to others.
Why does high efferent coupling often increase instability?	Because the artifact depends on many others and is more likely to break when they change.
What does abstractness measure?	The ratio of abstract artifacts to the total of abstract plus concrete artifacts in a codebase or module.
What does instability measure?	The ratio of outgoing coupling to total incoming plus outgoing coupling.
What does Distance from the Main Sequence measure?	How far a module is from the ideal balance between abstractness and instability.
What does the Zone of Uselessness represent?	Code that is too abstract and therefore hard to use productively.
What does the Zone of Pain represent?	Code that is too concrete and rigid, making it brittle and hard to maintain.
Why do architecture metrics still require interpretation?	Because they expose structural patterns but cannot fully distinguish good complexity from bad complexity or explain intent.
What is connascence?	A way of describing coupling where changing one element requires another to change to preserve system correctness.
What is the difference between static and dynamic connascence?	Static connascence appears in source structure, while dynamic connascence depends on runtime behavior or ordering.
What is Connascence of Name?	Multiple elements must agree on the same identifier or name.
What is Connascence of Type?	Multiple elements must agree on the same type.
What is Connascence of Meaning?	Multiple elements must agree on the meaning of a value or convention, such as a magic number or string.
What is Connascence of Position?	Multiple elements must agree on the ordering of values, such as positional parameters.
What is Connascence of Algorithm?	Multiple elements must agree on the same algorithm in order to work correctly together.
What is Connascence of Timing?	Correctness depends on elements happening at the right time relative to one another.
What is Connascence of Values?	Several values must change together to preserve correctness.
What is Connascence of Identity?	Multiple elements must reference the same specific entity.
Why is static connascence generally preferable to dynamic connascence?	Static forms are easier to analyze and refactor, while dynamic forms are usually more fragile and harder to reason about.
What does the strength of connascence describe?	How hard a form of coupling is to change or refactor.
What does the locality of connascence describe?	How close the coupled elements are to one another in the codebase or architecture.
What does the degree of connascence describe?	How many elements are affected by the coupling.
What is the Rule of Degree for connascence?	Refactor stronger forms of connascence into weaker forms when possible.
What is the Rule of Locality for connascence?	As distance between elements increases, prefer weaker forms of connascence.
What are the three general modularity guidelines based on connascence?	Minimize overall connascence; minimize connascence across boundaries; maximize connascence within boundaries.
Why is connascence useful to architects?	It gives more precise language for identifying, discussing, and refactoring different kinds of coupling.
