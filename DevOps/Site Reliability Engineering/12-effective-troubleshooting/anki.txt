What is troubleshooting best understood as?	An iterative hypothesis-testing process that uses observations plus system knowledge to narrow down likely causes.
Why is system knowledge important in troubleshooting?	It makes hypotheses more plausible, helps interpret signals correctly, and reduces wasted investigation time.
What is a common reasoning pitfall in troubleshooting?	Chasing improbable explanations, old favorite failures, or misleading correlations instead of starting with simpler likely causes.
Why is correlation not enough to prove a cause during troubleshooting?	Correlated events may share a hidden cause or coincide by chance rather than causing one another.
What should a good problem report include for troubleshooting?	The expected behavior, the actual behavior, and ideally a way to reproduce the issue.
Why is opening a bug for every issue useful in troubleshooting?	It creates a searchable record of symptoms, investigation, and remediation that others can follow later.
Why should triage come before root-cause analysis during an outage?	Stopping the bleeding protects users and the system, while root-causing can wait until the situation is stabilized.
Why are logs valuable in troubleshooting?	They reveal what a process was doing at a particular time and help reconstruct behavior that metrics alone cannot explain.
Why is adjustable logging verbosity useful in production troubleshooting?	It lets you inspect more detail when needed without always paying the cost of maximal logging.
Why is exposing current component state useful for troubleshooting?	It helps operators inspect configuration, recent requests, dependencies, and derived values without guessing from outside the system.
What does "simplify and reduce" mean in troubleshooting?	Narrow the problem by checking component boundaries, using controlled test inputs, and reducing the search space step by step.
Why is divide-and-conquer effective in troubleshooting distributed systems?	It lets you isolate which half or layer of the system is behaving incorrectly before drilling deeper.
Why is "what changed recently?" a productive troubleshooting heuristic?	Working systems often fail after a change in code, configuration, environment, or workload, so recent changes are good suspects.
Why should troubleshooting tests be ordered by likelihood and risk?	You usually learn fastest and safest by checking common low-risk explanations before rarer or riskier ones.
Why can active testing mislead troubleshooting?	Changing the system to test a hypothesis can alter later observations and create confounding side effects.
Why should troubleshooting notes be explicit and timestamped?	They prevent repeated work, preserve reasoning, support handoffs, and make postmortems easier to write.
Why are negative results valuable in troubleshooting and experiments?	They conclusively rule out possibilities, narrow the design space, and can save others from repeating the same dead ends.
Why can proving a single definitive root cause be difficult in production systems?	Failures often depend on multiple interacting factors and may be hard or unsafe to reproduce exactly.
What two design choices make troubleshooting easier across systems?	Strong observability from the start and well-understood, observable interfaces between components.
