What is the traditional sysadmin approach to service management?	Run services by assembling existing components, operating them manually, and growing the ops team as system complexity and load increase.
What is a major direct cost of the traditional sysadmin model?	Manual operations scale with system load, so staffing costs rise as the service grows.
What is a major indirect cost of a strict development/operations split?	Different incentives, vocabulary, and risk assumptions can create conflict, poor communication, and mistrust.
How does Google define Site Reliability Engineering?	SRE is what happens when you ask a software engineer to design an operations team.
What is the core idea behind the SRE staffing model?	Use engineers who can replace manual operations work with software and automation.
Why does Google hire strong software engineers into SRE?	So they can engineer systems that reduce or eliminate repetitive operational toil.
Why must an SRE team keep coding or it will drown?	Without engineering work, operational load grows with the service until the team scales linearly just to keep up.
What ops-work cap does Google place on SRE teams?	About 50% of total SRE time.
What happens when an SRE team spends too much time on operational work?	Google pushes some operational burden back to development or adds staffing rather than letting the team become pure ops.
Why does the SRE model avoid the usual dev/ops split pathology?	SRE shares software-engineering culture with development while still owning operational outcomes.
What is one major challenge of the SRE model?	Hiring is difficult because SRE requires both strong coding ability and useful systems knowledge.
What are the core areas an SRE team is generally responsible for?	Availability, latency, performance, efficiency, change management, monitoring, emergency response, and capacity planning.
Why is maintaining a durable focus on engineering a core SRE tenet?	Engineering effort is what reduces recurring operational work instead of merely absorbing it with more people.
How many significant events should an SRE typically handle per on-call shift?	At most about two per 8- to 12-hour shift.
Why is too much pager load dangerous for SRE teams?	Engineers become overwhelmed and lose time to investigate, learn from, and fix underlying problems.
Why should significant incidents have postmortems even if they never triggered a page?	They often reveal important monitoring gaps and systemic weaknesses.
What is a blameless postmortem?	An investigation focused on understanding causes and improving the system rather than blaming individuals.
Why is 100% availability usually the wrong reliability target?	Users cannot distinguish it from a slightly lower target, while the extra engineering cost is enormous.
What is an error budget?	The permitted amount of unreliability, equal to one minus the service's availability target.
Why do error budgets reduce conflict between product development and SRE?	They align both groups around maximizing feature velocity without exceeding an agreed reliability limit.
What should happen when a service exhausts its error budget?	Further risky changes should stop until reliability is brought back within target.
Why does the error-budget model make outages a managed trade-off instead of a pure failure?	It treats some unreliability as an expected cost of innovation within agreed limits.
What is an alert in SRE?	A signal that a human must take immediate action.
What is a ticket in SRE?	A signal that a human should take action, but not immediately.
Why is email-style “someone should look at this” monitoring considered flawed in SRE?	Monitoring should already decide whether human action is required instead of asking a person to interpret raw signals.
What is the key metric for emergency response effectiveness in SRE?	Mean time to repair (MTTR).
Why do playbooks improve incident response?	They reduce improvisation and help engineers respond faster and more consistently under pressure.
Why does SRE treat change management as a primary reliability concern?	Most outages are caused by changes to a live system.
What practices make change management safer in SRE?	Progressive rollouts, fast detection of bad changes, and safe rollback mechanisms.
Why does automation improve change management?	It reduces human error and makes rollout and rollback faster and more consistent.
Why is capacity planning an SRE responsibility?	Capacity is tightly coupled to availability, so the team responsible for reliability must plan and provision it.
What should capacity planning account for?	Both organic growth and inorganic demand such as launches or marketing events.
Why must provisioning be handled carefully?	It is expensive, risky, and directly affects whether capacity will work when needed.
Why are efficiency and performance part of the SRE mission?	SRE controls provisioning and helps shape software behavior, so it has major leverage over cost and service capacity.
What is a machine in Google's production terminology?	A piece of hardware, or sometimes a VM, that can run server software.
What is a server in Google's production terminology?	A piece of software that implements a service.
Why is software essential for managing hardware at Google's scale?	Hardware failures are frequent enough at scale that they must be abstracted and handled automatically.
What is Borg?	A distributed cluster operating system that schedules and manages jobs across a cluster.
What kinds of workloads can Borg run?	Long-running server processes and batch jobs such as MapReduce.
Why can Google not rely on fixed IP addresses and ports to identify tasks?	Tasks can be restarted or moved to different machines, so their network location is fluid.
What problem does the Borg Naming Service (BNS) solve?	It provides stable names for tasks and maps those names to the current IP address and port.
Why do jobs declare resource requirements to Borg?	So Borg can place tasks efficiently and safely across machines.
Why does Borg consider failure domains when placing tasks?	To avoid putting too many replicas behind the same shared point of failure, such as a rack switch.
What happens if a Borg task uses more resources than it requested?	Borg kills and restarts the task.
What is Colossus?	A cluster-wide filesystem layer over distributed storage that provides filesystem semantics plus replication and encryption.
What is Bigtable?	A distributed NoSQL database for very large datasets, modeled as a sparse sorted map.
What is Spanner?	A globally consistent distributed database with an SQL-like interface.
What is Google Front End (GFE)?	A reverse proxy that terminates client connections and routes requests to the appropriate backend service.
What does Global Software Load Balancing (GSLB) do?	It directs traffic to suitable locations with available capacity, including at DNS, service, and RPC levels.
What is Chubby used for in Google's production environment?	Distributed locking, master election, and storage of small pieces of consistent coordination data.
What is Borgmon?	A monitoring system that scrapes metrics for alerting and historical analysis.
What RPC system underlies service-to-service communication at Google?	Stubby, whose open source descendant is gRPC.
Why might Google use an RPC boundary even for work that could be a local call?	It makes later refactoring into a separate service easier.
Why are protocol buffers used for RPC payloads?	They serialize structured data compactly, quickly, and with less ambiguity than XML.
What is one operational advantage of a single shared repository?	Engineers can fix problems across project boundaries and submit those changes through the same review flow.
Why does Google run broad automated testing when a changelist is submitted?	To detect breakage in any code that depends directly or indirectly on the change.
What is push-on-green?	A release model that automatically pushes a new version to production after it passes tests.
Why can making a service more reliable eventually become worse rather than better?	Extreme reliability can slow feature delivery, raise costs sharply, and produce little user-visible benefit.
What are the two main cost dimensions of increasing reliability?	The cost of extra redundant resources and the opportunity cost of engineering time spent reducing risk instead of building user-facing features.
What does it mean for SRE to make a service "reliable enough, but no more"?	Align reliability with the business's actual risk tolerance instead of overinvesting in unnecessary availability.
Why does SRE treat the availability target as both a minimum and a maximum?	Falling short hurts users, but greatly exceeding the target can waste opportunities to ship features, reduce costs, or pay down technical debt.
What is time-based availability?	Availability measured as the proportion of time a system is up during a period.
Why is time-based availability often less meaningful for globally distributed Google services?	A service may still be serving some traffic somewhere even when part of it is impaired, so pure uptime does not reflect user experience well.
What is yield-based availability in SRE?	Availability measured as the proportion of successful requests or successful units of work.
Why does Google often measure availability as request success rate instead of uptime?	Request success rate better reflects user-visible reliability in globally distributed services that are rarely completely down everywhere.
Why can request-success-rate availability also work for nonserving systems?	Batch, pipeline, and storage systems usually still have a measurable notion of successful versus failed units of work.
What kinds of factors help determine the risk tolerance of a consumer service?	User expectations, revenue impact, whether the service is paid or free, competitor reliability, and whether the service targets consumers or enterprises.
Why can different failure types imply different risk tolerance decisions?	Some failures merely degrade experience, while others, such as privacy breaches, can damage trust so severely that full shutdown is preferable.
How can cost help determine the right availability target for a service?	A team can compare the value of one more increment of availability against the engineering and infrastructure cost required to achieve it.
Why might latency targets differ even between two advertising systems?	The acceptable latency depends on the surrounding user experience and business context, not on ads as a category.
Why might an infrastructure service need multiple levels of service?	Different clients may value latency, throughput, consistency, and cost differently.
How can the same infrastructure offer different service levels without different hardware?	By changing provisioning, redundancy, geographic constraints, and configuration to trade cost against latency or reliability.
What problem is an error budget meant to solve organizationally?	It gives product development and SRE a shared objective metric for negotiating release risk instead of relying on politics, fear, or habit.
How is an error budget formed from an SLO?	The SLO sets the target reliability, actual performance is measured, and the gap between perfect reliability and the target defines the allowed unreliability budget.
What release rule follows from the error-budget model?	As long as the service remains within its error budget, new releases may continue.
Why does an outage from a network or datacenter failure still consume error budget?	The whole team shares responsibility for availability, regardless of whether the cause was a release or infrastructure event.
How can loosening an SLO change product behavior?	It increases the error budget, allowing the team to take more release risk and move faster.
What is an SLI in SRE?	A carefully defined quantitative measure of some aspect of the service level being provided.
What is an SLO in SRE?	A target value or target range for a service level measured by an SLI.
What is an SLA in SRE?	An explicit or implicit agreement with users that includes consequences for meeting or missing service targets.
How can you usually tell the difference between an SLO and an SLA?	If missing the target has an explicit consequence, especially a business or financial one, it is an SLA.
Why is correctness still important even when it is not purely an SRE-owned concern?	A system can be available and fast yet still unhealthy if it returns the wrong answer or processes the wrong data.
Why is an SLI sometimes only a proxy for what users actually care about?	The most user-relevant behavior may be hard to measure directly, so teams approximate it with something measurable.
Why can client-side measurement be necessary in addition to server-side measurement?	Server metrics can miss user-visible problems such as frontend or browser-side latency.
Why should teams avoid choosing every possible metric as an SLI?	Too many indicators dilute attention and make it harder to focus on the behaviors that actually matter.
Why is averaging a metric over a long window potentially misleading?	It can hide short bursts, spikes, and other behaviors that matter operationally.
Why can average latency be a poor SLI summary?	Averages can hide long-tail requests that strongly affect user experience.
Why are percentiles usually better than means for latency SLIs?	Percentiles reveal the shape of the distribution, including tail behavior, instead of collapsing it into one average.
What does a high percentile such as the 99th or 99.9th latency represent?	A plausible worst-case latency that exposes long-tail behavior.
Why do some SRE teams focus on high-percentile latency rather than averages?	If tail latency is good, the typical experience is usually good as well, and users are sensitive to variance.
Why should SRE teams avoid assuming their operational data is normally distributed?	System metrics are often skewed or bounded, so standard intuitions about averages and outliers may fail.
Why should teams standardize SLI definitions?	Common templates reduce ambiguity and make SLI specifications easier to understand and compare.
What kinds of defaults can SLI templates standardize?	Measurement window, aggregation scope, sampling frequency, included requests, collection point, and latency definition.
Why is it often better to work backward from user goals to indicators?	Starting from what users care about produces more meaningful objectives than starting from whatever is easiest to measure.
Why should an SLO specify how it is measured and under what conditions it is valid?	Precise definitions remove ambiguity about what the target actually means.
When might it make sense to define multiple latency SLO targets instead of one?	When you need to capture different parts of the performance curve, such as typical, bad, and worst-case behavior.
When might it make sense to define separate SLOs for different workload classes?	When different clients value different things, such as throughput for batch users and latency for interactive users.
Why is 100% SLO compliance unrealistic and undesirable?	It reduces innovation, encourages overengineering, and often costs more than users need.
What is an error budget in the context of SLO management?	The allowed rate at which SLOs may be missed over a period.
Why shouldn't teams pick SLO targets based only on current system performance?	That can lock them into unrealistic or accidental targets based on today's implementation rather than user needs.
Why should SLOs be kept as simple as possible?	Complicated aggregations are harder to reason about and can hide meaningful changes in behavior.
Why should a service have as few SLOs as possible?	A small set of meaningful objectives is easier to prioritize around and more useful in real decisions.
Why is it better to start with a loose SLO and tighten it later than the reverse?	Relaxing an overly strict target is painful, while tightening a loose target is easier as you learn more.
How do SLOs function as control measures?	Teams monitor SLIs, compare them to targets, decide whether action is needed, and then take corrective action.
Why do published SLOs help users?	They set realistic expectations about how the service will behave and whether it fits the user's needs.
Why keep a tighter internal SLO than the one advertised externally?	It provides a safety margin so teams can respond to chronic problems before users notice.
Why should services avoid significantly overachieving their published SLOs?	Users will build dependencies on actual behavior rather than stated guarantees, creating future risk.
What is SRE's role in SLAs?	Help define measurable indicators and reduce the risk of missing targets, while business and legal teams own the agreement itself.
What is toil in SRE?	Operational work tied to running a service that is manual, repetitive, automatable, tactical, lacking enduring value, and that scales linearly with service growth.
What is overhead in SRE?	Administrative work not directly tied to running a production service, such as meetings, HR tasks, reviews, and training.
Why is some grungy work not considered toil?	If it creates a lasting improvement to the service, it has enduring value and is therefore engineering work rather than toil.
Why does manually running a script still count as toil?	The human hands-on time is still manual operational work even if the script automates the individual steps.
Why is interrupt-driven work usually toil?	It is reactive and tactical rather than strategic and proactive.
What does it mean for toil to have no enduring value?	After the task is finished, the service is in essentially the same state as before.
Why is work that scales linearly with service growth considered toil?	Healthy SRE practices aim for services to grow substantially without requiring proportional increases in manual work.
Why does Google aim to keep toil below 50% of an SRE's time?	SRE needs at least half of its time for engineering work that reduces future toil or improves the service.
Why does toil tend to expand if left unchecked?	Manual operational work naturally fills available time unless teams deliberately automate and eliminate it.
What organizational benefit comes from reducing toil?	The SRE organization can scale sublinearly with service size instead of growing like a traditional ops team.
Why is keeping the 50% engineering promise important to SRE hiring and morale?	SREs join expecting meaningful engineering work, and breaking that promise makes the role feel like ordinary ops.
Why does on-call create a floor on toil?	Primary and secondary on-call duties guarantee a baseline amount of interrupt handling and operational work.
What qualifies as engineering work in SRE?	Work that is novel, requires human judgment, follows a strategy, and creates a lasting improvement in the service.
What is software engineering work in SRE?	Writing or changing code, including automation, tooling, reliability features, and infrastructure code improvements.
What is systems engineering work in SRE?	One-time configuration or production-system improvements that create lasting benefits, such as monitoring, tuning, and productionization work.
Why is excessive toil dangerous for an individual SRE?	It causes career stagnation, low morale, boredom, and eventual burnout.
Why is excessive toil dangerous for the SRE organization?	It blurs the engineering identity of SRE, slows product progress, invites more operational dumping, and drives attrition.
What is monitoring in SRE?	Collecting, processing, aggregating, and displaying real-time quantitative data about a system.
What is white-box monitoring?	Monitoring based on internal system metrics and signals exposed by components such as logs, runtime interfaces, or metrics endpoints.
What is black-box monitoring?	Monitoring that tests externally visible system behavior as a user would experience it.
Why should paging alerts have high signal and low noise?	Frequent low-value pages cause alert fatigue, slower response, and missed real incidents.
What is the difference between a symptom and a cause in monitoring?	A symptom tells you what is broken for users; a cause suggests why it is broken.
Why is black-box monitoring especially valuable for paging?	It pages humans only when a real user-visible problem is actively happening.
Why is white-box monitoring especially valuable for debugging and prevention?	It can reveal internal failures, masked errors, and imminent problems before they become obvious to users.
What are the four golden signals of monitoring?	Latency, traffic, errors, and saturation.
What does latency measure in monitoring?	The time it takes to service a request.
Why should successful and failed request latency be tracked separately?	Fast failures can make overall latency look better even while users are experiencing broken requests.
What does traffic measure in monitoring?	The amount of demand being placed on a system, expressed in a system-appropriate rate such as requests per second.
What do errors measure in monitoring?	The rate of requests that fail explicitly, implicitly, or by violating an expected service policy such as a latency target.
What does saturation measure in monitoring?	How full the system is with respect to its most constrained resources and how close it is to degraded performance.
Why can high-percentile latency be an early warning sign of saturation?	Tail latency often rises before average utilization reaches 100 percent or before a system visibly fails.
Why is monitoring only the mean often misleading?	Averages can hide imbalanced resource usage and long tails that dominate user experience.
How do latency histograms help with monitoring?	They show the distribution of request times, making slow-tail behavior visible instead of collapsing everything into one average.
Why should measurement resolution vary by metric and use case?	Different signals change at different speeds, so overcollecting wastes resources while undercollecting hides important behavior.
How can internal sampling with later aggregation reduce monitoring cost?	It preserves high-resolution local observations while exporting only summarized distributions for storage and analysis.
What makes a good page in SRE?	It is urgent, actionable, requires human judgment, and points to a novel or unresolved problem.
Why are pages with rote, algorithmic responses a red flag?	If the response is predictable, it should usually be automated instead of interrupting a human.
What long-term trade-off should teams consider when designing monitoring?	A short-term drop in availability or sensitivity may be worthwhile if it reduces pager load and enables durable system improvements.
Why is automation described as a force multiplier rather than a panacea in SRE?	Automation amplifies whatever process it is applied to, so poor automation can create as many problems as it solves.
Why can automation be more valuable as a platform than as a one-off script?	A reusable platform centralizes fixes, can be extended, and can expose metrics and process details for further improvement.
How does automation help reduce MTTR for common faults?	It can detect and remediate routine failures faster than humans, shortening recovery time for recurring problems.
Why is faster action an important benefit of automation?	Machines can react immediately to well-defined failures, while waiting for humans can add unnecessary delay.
What does decoupling operator from operation mean?	Once a task is automated, the ability to run it no longer depends on a specific human performing it manually.
What is a key trade-off of higher-level automation abstractions?	They are easier to use and reason about, but when the abstraction leaks they can fail systemically in messy real-world conditions.
Why do real-world rollout and failover abstractions often leak?	Networks, machines, staging steps, and verification can fail in partial ways that simple abstractions do not model well.
Why is autonomous behavior preferable to external glue logic when possible?	A system designed to handle its own routine conditions is more robust than one that depends on separate automation to keep functioning.
Why is rarely executed automation especially fragile?	Infrequent execution means slower feedback, poorer testing, and more bit rot between uses.
Why should automation that applies fixes be idempotent?	It should be safe to run repeatedly without causing additional harm if retried or triggered again.
What organizational problem appears when service owners stop maintaining the automation for their own systems?	Automation becomes less accurate and relevant because the people closest to changing systems no longer keep it in sync.
Why are the most functional automation tools often written by the teams that use them?	The users best understand the real workflow, edge cases, and changing requirements the automation must handle.
What lesson did MySQL on Borg illustrate about automation?	When failures are inevitable, automation should optimize for fast recovery rather than assuming failures can be avoided.
What broader lesson did Borg illustrate about automation?	The highest leverage comes from redesigning systems to be autonomous rather than layering manual or external automation on top of fragile workflows.
Why is self-introspection important for autonomous systems?	A system needs awareness of its own state and failures in order to repair or adapt itself without waiting for human intervention.
Why is reliability described as the fundamental feature behind automation choices?	The goal is not automation for its own sake, but resilient systems that continue operating and recover safely at scale.
What risk does highly effective automation create for human operators?	If humans stop interacting with the system, they may lose the skill and mental model needed to manage it when automation fails.
What failure pattern does "automation enabling failure at scale" describe?	A bug in powerful automation can rapidly spread a mistake across huge portions of production.
What safeguards help limit automation-caused disasters at scale?	Idempotent workflows, sanity checks, auditability, and rate limiting help stop bad automation from propagating unchecked.
