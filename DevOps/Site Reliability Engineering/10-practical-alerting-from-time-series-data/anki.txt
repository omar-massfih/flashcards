Why is time-series data a strong foundation for alerting at scale?	It lets teams aggregate many low-level signals, reason over history, and build alerts from the same data used for dashboards and debugging.
Why is alerting on single-machine failures usually unacceptable at Google scale?	Single-machine signals are too noisy to be actionable in systems that should already tolerate routine component failures.
Why did time-series monitoring replace many custom check scripts?	A shared metrics format lowers collection overhead, avoids repeated subprocess work, and makes alerting and charting use the same underlying data.
Why does a common metrics exposition format help monitoring scale?	It enables mass collection with low overhead and makes rules reusable across many similar targets.
Why does a schemaless textual metrics interface make instrumentation easier to adopt?	Developers can add new metrics with very low friction because they do not need to coordinate on a rigid schema first.
What maintenance trade-off comes with decoupling metric definitions from alert rules?	Renaming or changing metrics becomes riskier because rule correctness depends on separate configuration staying in sync.
Why are synthetic collection variables useful in a monitoring system?	They make collection failures, name-resolution failures, and health-check failures visible as first-class signals.
Why are labels so important in time-series monitoring?	They let teams slice, group, and aggregate metrics across dimensions such as instance, job, service, code, shard, or location.
Why is aggregation the cornerstone of alerting in distributed systems?	It turns many task-level signals into service-level indicators that are stable enough to alert on.
Why are counters usually better than gauges for sampled monitoring?	Counters preserve the total amount of work between samples, while gauges can miss transient activity that happens between collections.
Why is summing rates often safer than taking the rate of a summed counter?	Summing per-series rates is more robust to counter resets, restarts, and missing samples.
Why is it useful that monitoring rules create new derived time-series?	The computed results can be inspected later as charts or queried during debugging instead of disappearing after alert evaluation.
Why should an alert condition usually hold for multiple evaluation cycles before firing?	This reduces flapping and prevents transient or missed collections from causing false pages.
What is the role of an alert manager in a monitoring system?	It routes, deduplicates, inhibits, and groups alert notifications before sending them to people or ticket systems.
Why is a hierarchical monitoring topology useful?	It scales collection and aggregation, avoids a single global bottleneck, and preserves drill-down visibility through multiple layers.
Why is black-box probing still necessary even with strong white-box monitoring?	White-box metrics can miss user-visible failures that happen before requests ever reach the target or that only appear from the outside.
Why should monitoring rules be separated from target lists?	Reusing the same rule logic across many targets reduces repetition and lowers configuration maintenance cost.
Why should monitoring configurations have automated tests?	Synthetic and regression tests catch rule mistakes early and verify that alert logic behaves as intended before rollout.
