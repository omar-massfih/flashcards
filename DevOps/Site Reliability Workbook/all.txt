Why are SLOs culturally important in SRE?	They turn reliability into a shared stakeholder decision and make reliability discussions data-driven rather than blame-driven.

Why are ratio-style SLIs useful?	They map cleanly to 0%-100%, make error budgets easy to calculate, and allow common tooling for alerting and reporting.
What is the difference between an SLI specification and an SLI implementation?	The specification states the user-relevant outcome to measure; the implementation states exactly how that outcome is measured.
Why can one SLI specification have multiple implementations?	Different measurement points trade off quality, coverage, and cost while trying to approximate the same user outcome.
Why is it acceptable for the first SLO attempt to be imperfect?	The main goal is to start measuring and create a feedback loop for later refinement.

Why can client-side instrumentation improve SLI quality?	It measures closer to the real user experience, including problems outside the backend's view.

What is the goal of SLO-based alerting?	Notify an on-call engineer about significant events before they consume too much of the error budget.
What makes an event significant for SLO-based alerting?	It consumes a large enough fraction of the error budget to require action.

Why is alerting when the recent error rate exceeds the SLO threshold usually too noisy?	Small, brief violations can trigger alerts even when they consume only a tiny fraction of the error budget.
Why does increasing the alert window improve precision?	It requires an elevated error rate to persist long enough to represent a larger error-budget spend.
What is the main drawback of a very long alert window?	It creates poor reset time because the long-window average stays high long after the incident ends.
Why can long-window alert calculations be expensive?	They require evaluating many data points over a large time range.
Why should SLO-based alerting usually avoid simple duration clauses?	They do not scale with incident severity and can reset whenever the metric briefly returns to a passing value.

What is burn rate in SLO alerting?	How fast the service is consuming error budget relative to the rate that would exactly exhaust the budget over the SLO window.
What is the main weakness of a single burn-rate alert?	It can miss lower but sustained burn rates that still exhaust the error budget.

When is a ticket more appropriate than a page for SLO-based alerting?	When the budget burn is significant but slow enough to handle during normal working time.
What problem does multiwindow multi-burn-rate alerting solve?	It preserves detection of significant budget burn while reducing false positives and improving reset time.
How does multiwindow multi-burn-rate alerting work?	It requires both a long window and a shorter window to exceed the same burn-rate threshold before firing.
Why does adding a short window improve reset time?	The alert stops once the short-window error rate returns below threshold, even if the long-window average remains elevated.
Why is multiwindow multi-burn-rate alerting generally recommended?	It gives good precision and recall while letting teams tune alert severity by budget burn speed.

Why should automation avoid relying on expert technicians to catch errors?	Expert judgment is hard to replicate and new users may use tools in unsafe ways the system did not anticipate.
Why is repair automation not "fire and forget"?	It can outlive original assumptions, require ongoing maintenance, and become brittle unless designed to evolve.
Why can policy-based automation be useful?	It separates intent from the implementation engine, making long-lived automation easier to evolve.
Why should automation projects have a failure budget?	New automation can fail at first, so stakeholders need a shared tolerance that protects the project while it improves.
Why did the decommissioning effort proceed in phases?	Phases reduced user disruption, let the team build tools as needed, and increased confidence in alternatives over time.
What does "melt snowflakes" mean in toil reduction?	Modify or remove special cases so reality better matches automation assumptions.
How can organizational nudges reduce future toil?	Escalations, recognition, documentation, and onboarding guidance steer users toward better low-toil alternatives.
Why is simplicity a reliability goal for SREs?	Simple systems tend to break less often and are easier to understand, maintain, test, and repair.
Why should SREs treat simplicity as an end-to-end property?	Operational complexity can appear in architecture, configuration, tooling, deployment, and processes, not only in source code.
Why is measuring system-level complexity harder than measuring code complexity?	Systems have many interacting components, configurations, dependencies, and operational paths that do not reduce cleanly to one code metric.
How can training time and explanation time proxy for system complexity?	They estimate how difficult the system is to learn, operate, and explain at a high level.
What does administrative diversity reveal about system complexity?	How many different ways the system configures or manages similar settings.
What does diversity of deployed configurations reveal about system complexity?	How many distinct production binaries, versions, flags, and environments operators must reason about.
Why can system age increase complexity?	Over time, users may come to depend on incidental behaviors, making changes more fragile and unpredictable.
What does Hyrum's Law warn about?	With enough users, every observable behavior of an API may become a dependency, even if it was not promised.
Why is complexity often an externality?	The person or team adding complexity may not bear the full long-term cost of operating and understanding it.
Why are SREs natural champions for end-to-end simplicity?	SRE work requires a whole-system view across services, dependencies, operations, and production failure modes.
Why should new on-call engineers draw system diagrams?	Diagramming builds a shared mental model of system interactions and exposes gaps in understanding.
Why keep canonical system diagrams in documentation?	They help new engineers learn the system and help experienced engineers track architectural changes.
Why should SREs review major design documents?	SREs can identify operational complexity early and suggest simpler designs before the complexity is built.
Why can structured RPC schemas be simpler than flexible untyped APIs?	They force upfront design decisions and make parameters, types, and compatibility more explicit.
What lifecycle costs should teams consider before rewriting a system?	Development against a moving target, migration effort, dual-system maintenance, and the opportunity cost of not improving the current system.
What comparison should teams make when evaluating a rewrite?	Compare the rewrite outcome to what the existing system could become if the same effort were invested in improving it.
Why are wide APIs with many users hard to replace?	Many clients, configurations, and workflows must migrate, and some may depend on undocumented behavior.
Why should simplification be prioritized like feature work?	It delivers operator and user value by reducing cognitive load, but it needs explicit time and rewards or feature work will crowd it out.
Why can splitting SRE teams make simplification harder?	Narrower team scope can reduce ownership of cross-stack consistency and end-to-end simplification.
How can system diagrams reveal reliability risks?	They make dependencies, request paths, amplification, and cycles visible enough to reason about.
What is request amplification?	A failure or timeout causes retries at multiple layers, multiplying the total number of requests.
Why are cyclic dependencies dangerous in production systems?	They can compromise system integrity and may make a full cold start impossible.
Why are redundant lookups in one request a system smell?	They add unnecessary latency, capacity demand, and complexity to the request path.
Why should acquired or independently built systems be integrated incrementally?	Incremental integration reduces migration risk and lets teams standardize without a disruptive big-bang rewrite.
How do shared production standards reduce complexity?	They establish common ways to configure, monitor, provision, and perform common operational tasks.
Why do standards need buy-in from SREs and developers?	Shared agreement makes the simplification plan credible, adoptable, and easier for managers to support.
Why can a shared microservices platform simplify operations?	It replaces many bespoke production stacks with common workflows, tools, defaults, and reliability features.
Why should shared platforms provide incremental productivity wins?	Teams are more likely to adopt a standard platform when each migration step pays off before the full transition is complete.
Why distinguish managed services from merely hosted services?	Managed platforms should empower service teams with reliable workflows and tools rather than removing all responsibility from them.
How can standard platforms change SRE engagement?	They can let many services run with lighter consulting or design-review support instead of deep SRE on-call involvement.
Why use dependency allowlists for critical infrastructure services?	They prevent accidental new dependencies and keep critical service paths easier to reason about.
Why are self-dependencies especially risky?	They may work during normal replicated operation but fail catastrophically during cold start or recovery.
What is a practical way to break a circular service dependency?	Move the bootstrap data or lookup path to a lower-level local mechanism that does not depend on the service being resolved.
What is the core idea of SRE practices?	Apply software engineering solutions to operational problems.
How do SRE practices turn operational problems into engineering work?	They automate, redesign, or algorithmically manage work that would otherwise require manual operations.
What are the two main categories of SRE team work?	Operational work and project work.
Why is documentation not treated as a separate SRE work category?	Healthy documentation is embedded into project and operational tasks rather than treated as separate work.
What project-work target does Google set for SREs?	At least 50% of SRE time should be spent on project work.
Why should SRE teams periodically review their work balance?	Tracking work mix over time helps identify unhealthy operational load, underload, or lack of engineering capacity.
What is the purpose of Production Excellence reviews?	To give SRE leadership a rubric-based view of team health and production practices over time.
What is operational underload?	A state where engineers spend so little time operating a service that they lose practical knowledge of it.
How can teams counter operational underload?	Take more operationally useful risks, move faster, run disaster recovery tests, onboard related services, or hand back services that no longer need SRE support.
How should operational work feed project work?	Operational pain, toil, incidents, and requests should identify strategic projects that improve production systematically.
What kinds of SRE projects can reduce operational load?	More robust storage, less brittle frameworks, safer rollouts, sharding, automation, and fixes for systemic outage causes.
What is the main goal of a healthy on-call rotation?	Provide coverage for critical services without sacrificing the health of on-call engineers.
Why should on-call load leave room for project work?	Project work lets the team strategically fix production problems and reduce future operational load.
What pager-load target does Google use for on-call shifts?	At most two distinct incidents per 12-hour on-call shift.
Why is psychological safety important for on-call rotations?	On-call work is stressful, so engineers need procedures, escalation paths, and support to respond confidently.
Why should out-of-hours on-call work be compensated?	Compensation recognizes the burden, creates an incentive to participate, and discourages excessive shift-taking.
Why is a training roadmap useful before a team starts on-call?	It defines the service knowledge and operational skills engineers need before taking production responsibility.
What skills should on-call training exercises build?	Confidence and muscle memory for common debugging, mitigation, rollback, traffic draining, capacity, and monitoring tasks.
Why are service deep dives useful for new on-call engineers?	They teach engineers to explore architecture, running jobs, dependencies, monitoring, and recent incidents from first principles.
Why should new on-call engineers practice with mock incidents?	Role-playing recent failures builds debugging skill and psychological safety before real pages arrive.
What should an on-call handoff include?	The previous shift's active issues, recent pages, mitigations, follow-up needs, and anything the next on-caller must watch.
What should an on-call engineer prioritize during an incident?	Minimize user impact first, then ensure the issue is fully addressed and followed up.
What is the purpose of an alert playbook?	Give high-level guidance on severity, user impact, debugging, mitigation, and resolution for a specific alert.
Why should every paging alert have a playbook entry?	Playbooks reduce stress, MTTR, and human error when an alert fires.
What is the trade-off between general and step-by-step playbooks?	General playbooks stay current longer; step-by-step playbooks reduce variability and MTTR but go stale faster.
When should a playbook procedure become automation?	When it is a deterministic list of commands the on-caller runs every time the alert fires.
Why are SLOs useful when redesigning on-call alerts?	They focus pages on user-impacting symptoms rather than low-level infrastructure noise.
Why can one incident create multiple pages?	Several alerts can fire for the same underlying problem, so incident count and page count are not always identical.
Why should response-time expectations vary by incident severity?	Not every issue requires an engineer to be at a computer within minutes; urgency should match user and business impact.
When should an issue page a human instead of becoming a ticket?	When it is urgent, immediately actionable, and cannot be handled safely by automation or normal-hours follow-up.
How can teams reduce bugs before they reach production?	Improve tests, use load testing, run production-like staging, canary releases, and learn from each production bug.
Why is load testing important for reducing pager load?	Some bugs appear only under particular traffic levels or request mixes, not in ordinary functional tests.
Why prefer "detect, roll back, fix, and roll forward" for new bugs?	It removes user impact quickly and avoids continuing to expose production to a known bad change.
Why do frequent small releases reduce identification delay?	They make it easier to correlate a new page with the specific change that introduced the bug.
How can searchable change logs reduce identification delay?	They help correlate alerts with recent code, configuration, or infrastructure changes.
Why should on-call engineers ask for help early?	Faster collaboration reduces identification delay and reinforces that the on-caller is not alone.
Why does mitigation delay increase pager load?	The longer a bug remains in production after identification, the more chances it has to recur and page again.
How can feature flags reduce mitigation delay?	They let teams disable a broken feature without disabling unrelated functionality or waiting for a full rollback.
How can draining requests reduce mitigation delay?	Traffic can be redirected away from affected instances or clusters quickly while rollback or repair proceeds.
Why are non-rollbackable changes dangerous for on-call health?	They make mitigation slower and riskier, increasing user impact and pager load.
What makes a paging alert high quality?	It is immediately actionable, has a strong signal-to-noise ratio, and corresponds to meaningful user or SLO impact.
How should teams test new alerts before paging on them?	Run them in nonpaging mode long enough to observe normal production cycles and estimate false positives and pager-budget impact.
Why is relaxing SLO-based alert thresholds rarely the right response to paging?	If alerts are symptom-based and tied to real error-budget burn, the system needs stability work rather than less sensitive paging.
Why should teams identify the root cause of every page?	Knowing the cause lets teams prevent recurrence instead of leaving the next on-caller to face the same issue.
Why is "transient" a weak explanation for a page?	It often masks an unidentified cause and invites the same bug to page again later.
What should teams do when a page's root cause is unknown?	Add monitoring, logging, or investigation notes that make the next occurrence easier to diagnose.
What is the difference between a point fix and a systemic fix?	A point fix addresses one instance of a bug; a systemic fix prevents a class of similar bugs.
Why should page follow-up look for systemic fixes?	A page is a chance to remove an entire class of future failures, not just close the immediate incident.
How can teams justify a larger production-health project from pages?	Compare implementation cost with the number of pages and handling hours the project would eliminate.
Why should on-call data be tied to bugs or issues?	Structured data reveals which bugs, components, and processes cause the most pages and deserve priority.
What does high-quality pager-load data enable?	Data-driven prioritization, known-issue tracking, automated reports, and better production-health decisions.
How can teams maintain data quality for paging events?	Document expectations, alert on missing follow-up metadata, and reinforce good linking between pages, bugs, and handoffs.
Why should teams watch pager-load trends over time?	Operational overload often accumulates gradually, so trend review catches unhealthy load before burnout.
Why should on-call shifts often be limited to 12 hours when pages are frequent?	Shorter shifts reduce exhaustion and mistakes, making on-call more sustainable.
Why is 24-hour continuous on-call usually unhealthy?	Engineers cannot reliably produce high-quality work while continuously responsible for pages without reprieve.
Why should on-call scheduling support flexibility?	Team members' life circumstances change, and healthy rotations must preserve coverage while staying fair and humane.
What properties should automated on-call scheduling support?	Changing constraints, load rebalancing, fairness, personal preferences, historical load, and stable already-published schedules.
Why should teams plan for long-term breaks from on-call?	People may need temporary relief for burnout or personal circumstances without overloading the rest of the rotation.
Why should SREs own production health rather than act only as operational helpers?	Ownership lets SREs define reliability roadmaps, drive resolution, maintain alerts, and improve the site strategically.
How can SREs build ownership while collaborating with feature developers?	They can implement reliability fixes themselves and send code reviews to subject-matter experts.
Why do positive team relationships improve on-call outcomes?	Engineers are more likely to fix bugs, complete action items, and avoid paging colleagues unnecessarily.
What should teams do when on-call is drowning in alerts?	Step back, collect data, compare with partner teams, and address pager-load causes systematically.
What is incident resolution?	Mitigating impact and/or restoring the affected service to its previous condition.
What is incident management?	Coordinating responders efficiently and keeping communication flowing among responders and stakeholders.
Why should incident response be structured before an outage?	Pre-agreed communication and coordination rules reduce chaos and let responders focus on mitigation.
What are core principles of incident response?	Maintain a clear line of command; define roles clearly; keep a working record; declare incidents early and often.
What is the Incident Command System?	A standardized framework for communication, command, and role assignment during emergencies.
What are the three Cs of incident management?	Coordinate response effort; communicate with responders and stakeholders; maintain control over the response.
What usually explains failures in incident response?	A breakdown in coordination, communication, or control.
What is the Incident Commander role?	The person responsible for leading, coordinating, delegating, and maintaining control of the incident response.
What roles does the Incident Commander own by default?	Any incident-response roles that have not yet been delegated.
What is the Operations Lead role?	The person responsible for applying operational tools and coordinating technical work to mitigate or resolve the incident.
What is the Communications Lead role?	The person responsible for status updates, stakeholder communication, and managing inquiries about the incident.
Why declare incidents early?	Early declaration brings structure, reduces miscommunication, loops in the right teams sooner, and usually speeds resolution.
Why should incident responders use one shared communication location?	It reduces lost information, duplicated work, and conflicting assumptions during the response.
Why should teams avoid relying on heroic individual effort during incidents?	Heroics are unreliable, harm work-life balance, and fail if the needed people are unreachable.
What should teams do after mitigating an incident without knowing the root cause?	Continue investigating and prevent recurrence before resuming risky activity such as rollout expansion.
What order should active incident response usually follow?	Assess impact; mitigate impact; analyze root cause; then fix causes and write a postmortem after the incident.
Why is assessing customer impact early important?	It determines severity, communication needs, escalation urgency, and whether the issue is truly an incident.
Why should an Incident Commander establish formal structure as an incident grows?	More responders create communication overhead, so explicit roles and decision flow prevent confusion.
When is it useful to hand off Incident Commander duties?	When another responder has better incident-management experience, context, availability, or location coverage.
Why is a collaborative incident document useful with several responders?	It preserves theories, eliminated causes, logs, graphs, decisions, and debugging notes that can get lost in chat.
What should chat be used for during a large incident?	Decision making, coordination, summaries, and routing work, while detailed debugging can move to a shared document.
Why should Incident Commanders assign owners to mitigation options?	Ownership lets multiple approaches proceed in parallel without ambiguity about who is doing what.
What is a generic mitigation?	A broad preplanned action that reduces user pain before the exact root cause is fully understood.
Why are generic mitigations valuable?	They can stop the bleeding quickly while responders continue investigating the precise cause.
What are examples of generic mitigations?	Rolling back a release, draining traffic from a region, disabling a feature flag, or routing around a failing dependency.
What is the risk of generic mitigations?	They are blunt tools and may cause broader disruption than a precise fix.
When should teams build general-purpose mitigation tools?	Before incidents occur, using past postmortems to identify useful rollback, drain, or recovery capabilities.
Why do complex systems make incident response harder?	No single person may understand every interaction, so coordination and specialist escalation become critical.
Why are documented escalation paths important?	They help responders quickly bring in the right support, development, infrastructure, or communications experts.
What does successful incident response delegation look like?	Clear objectives are assigned to appropriate leads, who report progress back through the incident structure.
Why should the Incident Commander focus on customer impact?	The response should prioritize reducing harm to affected users over satisfying technical curiosity.
What audiences might need incident communication?	Responders, company leaders, dependent internal teams, external customers, support teams, and specific customers with open tickets.
Why should communications include regular status updates?	Without updates, people may assume nothing is being done or that the incident is still ongoing after resolution.
Why should teams prepare public communication templates?	Templates reduce stress and speed accurate announcements during high-pressure incidents.
Why should teams prepare contact lists before incidents?	Prebuilt lists save time and reduce the chance of missing important stakeholders or experts.
Why should teams define incident criteria beforehand?	Clear criteria help responders decide when to escalate from ordinary troubleshooting to formal incident response.
What is incident response training for?	It gives responders a shared pattern, language, and expectations to follow during real emergencies.
What should incident response training teach on-call engineers?	How to delegate, escalate, prioritize mitigation, and work within Incident Commander, Operations Lead, and Communications Lead roles.
Why should incident response training use real scenarios?	Concrete incidents help people connect theory to actions they may need to take under stress.
Why are incident response drills important?	They build habits and muscle memory before a high-stakes emergency occurs.
What is disaster recovery testing in incident response practice?	A controlled emergency exercise where teams respond as if a real incident were happening without customer impact.
What is a low-stakes way to practice major incident response?	Treat selected minor problems as formal incidents requiring structured coordination and communication.
How can postmortems improve incident response readiness?	They reveal recurring failure modes, useful mitigations, tooling gaps, and scenarios for future drills.
How can simulation exercises improve incident response teamwork?	They let responders practice roles, terminology, coordination, and escalation before real pressure hits.
Why can time-bound simulation games help incident training?	They recreate urgency and communication stress in a safer environment.
Why should major incident roles rotate during long incidents?	Rotation reduces fatigue, helps responders rest, and can bring fresh ideas into the response.
Why record major incident calls or keep detailed logs?	They help reconstruct timelines and learn from real-time communication after the incident.
What tools commonly support incident response?	On-call ownership data, a shared chat channel, conference calls, incident metadata, and postmortem records.
What advantage does practiced incident response provide?	It can shave minutes or hours from response time and helps teams stay calm under pressure.
Why does incident response structure matter more as incidents grow?	Larger incidents involve more unfamiliar collaborators, so procedures provide the coordination needed to move quickly.
