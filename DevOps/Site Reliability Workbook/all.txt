What does it mean to say SRE implements DevOps?	DevOps is a broad philosophy for improving operations through collaboration and culture, while SRE is a more concrete role and practice set that implements many of those ideas.
Why is treating operations purely as a cost center harmful?	It discourages investment in practices and engineering that improve reliability, speed, and long-term business outcomes.
Why are rigid silos harmful in operations work?	They encourage local optimization, knowledge isolation, handoff blame, and weak feedback between design and production.
What does the "accidents are normal" idea imply for operations culture?	Failures should be understood as system outcomes with missing safeguards, not mainly as individual mistakes to punish.
Why is blame counterproductive after operational incidents?	It encourages hiding, confusion, and defensiveness instead of accurate learning and faster future recovery.
Why does DevOps favor small frequent changes?	Smaller changes are easier to test, understand, roll back, and learn from than large batched changes.
Why is measurement crucial to DevOps and SRE?	It creates an objective basis for understanding reality, verifying improvement, and aligning conversations across functions.
How does SRE differ from DevOps in scope?	DevOps is a broad cultural and organizational philosophy, while SRE is a more service-oriented, opinionated operational discipline with specific practices.
Why does SRE frame operations as a software problem?	Because recurring operational work can often be reduced or eliminated through software engineering, automation, and better system design.
Why are SLOs culturally important in SRE?	They turn reliability into a shared stakeholder decision and make reliability discussions data-driven rather than blame-driven.
Why does SRE treat toil as something to minimize rather than as the job itself?	Manual repetitive operations consume the engineering time needed to make services more reliable and scalable.
What is the wisdom of production?	The practical understanding gained from observing how a system actually behaves under real production load, incidents, tickets, and user conditions.
Why can too little operational exposure be a problem for SREs?	Without enough contact with production reality, engineers may lose the feedback needed to design and operate the service well.
Why is the 50% toil cap better understood as a guarantee?	It guarantees space for engineering work that creates lasting operational improvement instead of letting toil consume the whole role.
What is the Murphy-Beyer effect in SRE toil?	As automatable work disappears, the remaining toil is often the work that is hardest or least attractive to automate.
How can reducing MTTR increase product development velocity?	Faster recovery and earlier problem discovery reduce the cleanup and interruption costs that otherwise slow feature work.
Why is shared ownership important between SRE and product development?	It prevents a hard split between building and operating, aligns incentives, and spreads authority to fix production problems.
Why should product developers and SREs use the same operational tooling?	Divergent tools create inconsistent behavior, duplicated effort, and weaker shared understanding of how the service is managed.
Why does SRE sometimes support CI/CD for different reasons than DevOps?	DevOps often emphasizes business flow and collaboration, while SRE emphasizes the operational reliability benefits of smaller tested changes.
Why are narrow reliability or launch incentives risky?	People can optimize the metric locally or game it unintentionally, harming the broader goal of reliable product velocity.
Why does early SRE engagement improve product outcomes?	Reliability issues are cheaper to fix during design and build than after operational ownership has shifted.
Why can withdrawal or delay of operational support be useful?	It creates an incentive to fix services that are too operationally costly instead of normalizing endless toil.
Why does reliability work need career and financial parity with product development?	Without comparable esteem and incentives, organizations struggle to attract and retain people with the specialized skills needed for reliable operations.
Why are SLOs central to SRE practice?	They turn reliability into a measurable target that can drive prioritization, alerting, risk management, and engineering trade-offs.
Why are SLOs useful for deciding reliability work priority?	They estimate which work most improves user experience or protects the error budget, rather than relying on intuition alone.
What organizational state is needed for error-budget-based SRE?	Stakeholders must approve fit-for-purpose SLOs, operators must believe they are defensible, the organization must use the budget for decisions, and there must be a refinement process.
Why is an SLO without an error budget policy weak?	It becomes a reporting metric rather than a rule that changes prioritization and risk decisions.
Why is 100% reliability usually the wrong SLO target?	It is unachievable across real dependencies, expensive beyond user value, blocks useful change, and forces purely reactive operations.
Who should own trade-offs once an SLO is below 100%?	Someone empowered to balance feature velocity and reliability, often the product owner or product manager.
Why are ratio-style SLIs useful?	They map cleanly to 0%-100%, make error budgets easy to calculate, and allow common tooling for alerting and reporting.
What is the difference between an SLI specification and an SLI implementation?	The specification states the user-relevant outcome to measure; the implementation states exactly how that outcome is measured.
Why can one SLI specification have multiple implementations?	Different measurement points trade off quality, coverage, and cost while trying to approximate the same user outcome.
Why is it acceptable for the first SLO attempt to be imperfect?	The main goal is to start measuring and create a feedback loop for later refinement.
When can current performance be a reasonable starting point for SLOs?	When no better user-satisfaction data exists and the team has a process for revisiting and refining the target.
Why should initial SLIs be simple and easy to measure?	Low-cost measurements help teams start learning quickly, and they can be refined as the SLO process matures.
How should a team start choosing SLIs for a service?	Pick one application, identify the users, list common and critical interactions, and draw the main request, data, and dependency flows.
Why should early SLOs use only a small number of SLI types?	A few critical user-facing measures are easier to reason about and act on than a broad set of diluted metrics.
Why can multiple latency SLO thresholds be useful?	They capture both typical experience and tail latency, so a small slow cohort is not hidden by a good median or broad pass rate.
What are common SLIs for request-driven services?	Availability, latency, and quality of undegraded responses.
What are common SLIs for data pipelines?	Freshness, correctness, and coverage of processed data.
What are common SLIs for storage systems?	Durability and successful later retrieval of written data, ideally scoped to the data users actually need.
Why must durability SLIs be scoped carefully?	A system can preserve almost all historical records while the specific recent records users need are unavailable.
Why might load balancer metrics be better than application-server logs for API SLIs?	They are often already available and can measure closer to the user's path than backend-only logs.
Why can server-side measurements miss user-facing failures?	Requests that fail before reaching the backend are invisible to server logs.
Why can client-side instrumentation improve SLI quality?	It measures closer to the real user experience, including problems outside the backend's view.
What is the cost of client-side SLI instrumentation?	It requires code changes and reliable telemetry infrastructure, which adds engineering and operational work.
Why should SLO time windows be chosen deliberately?	The window affects whether the SLO supports fast tactical corrections, strategic planning, or alignment with user memory and business cycles.
What is a benefit of a rolling SLO window?	It better matches user experience because incidents do not reset psychologically at calendar boundaries.
What is a benefit of a calendar-aligned SLO window?	It aligns better with business planning, quarterly reviews, and project allocation cycles.
Who needs to agree on a proposed SLO?	Product managers, product developers, and the team responsible for defending the production SLO.
What should an error budget policy define?	The actions to take when budget is exhausted or nearly exhausted, who owns them, and how disagreements are escalated.
Why is stakeholder disagreement about enforcing an error budget a signal to revisit the policy?	It means the SLO or policy may not be fit for purpose, even if the budget calculation is technically correct.
What should an SLO document include?	Authors, reviewers, approvers, review dates, service context, objectives, SLI implementations, budget calculation, and rationale for the numbers.
Why are SLO dashboards and reports useful?	They provide snapshots and trends for communicating compliance, budget burn, and problem areas across teams.
How can error budget consumption help prioritize incidents?	Incidents can be ranked by how much budget they consumed, highlighting which problems most affected reliability targets.
How can support tickets help improve SLO quality?	Comparing ticket spikes with budget loss reveals whether SLIs capture real user-facing pain.
What does it mean if incidents are not reflected in SLIs or SLO misses?	The SLO likely lacks coverage and should be refined.
What does it mean if SLO misses do not correlate with user-facing issues?	The SLO may be too sensitive or the SLI implementation may be a poor proxy for user experience.
How can a team improve a weak SLI implementation?	Measure closer to the user or increase coverage of real user interactions.
What is an aspirational SLO?	A tighter target that is measured and tracked but does not yet trigger the error budget policy.
When is an aspirational SLO useful?	When the desired target reflects user needs but the service cannot meet it yet without staying permanently in emergency mode.
How can SLOs guide project choice between reliability investments?	Compare expected error-budget savings from each project and prioritize the one with the greatest user-impact reduction.
Why can rare severe outages be less important than frequent bad releases?	Frequent smaller incidents may consume more total error budget over time than rare dramatic failures.
When might an SRE team step back from a service?	If the service is reliably within SLO, low toil, and users are satisfied, close daily SRE involvement may no longer be the best use of engineering time.
Why are critical user journeys harder to measure than simple request SLIs?	They span multiple steps and failure modes, so success may require joining logs, probes, client instrumentation, or custom telemetry.
Why can request bucketing improve SLO usefulness?	Different request classes, users, or tiers can have different reliability expectations and deserve different objectives.
Why can per-customer SLO tracking be noisy?	Customers with very low traffic can swing between perfect and poor measured availability because one failure is a large fraction of their requests.
How should dependencies influence SLO design?	Critical dependencies should have reliability targets compatible with the user journey, or the service must engineer around their limitations.
Why is simple availability multiplication often misleading for dependency modeling?	It assumes independent failures, but real systems share dependencies, control planes, failure domains, and operational fate.
How should error budget policy treat dependency-caused outages?	The chapter favors protecting users by reducing risk after any budget-consuming outage, even if the immediate cause was another team's dependency.
What is the trade-off of deliberately relaxing SLOs in an experiment?	It can reveal how reliability affects business outcomes, but it intentionally degrades user experience and can be misread if the chosen business metric misses latent dissatisfaction.
Why did Evernote adopt an SLO-centric SRE approach after trying other ops/dev models?	It wanted a shared, less subjective framework that balanced operations and development without pretending they were the same discipline.
Why was Evernote drawn to the SRE model instead of forcing ops and development into one role?	It accepted their different specialties while giving both teams a common way to reason about reliability trade-offs.
Why did Evernote start its SLO journey with uptime rather than a richer metric set?	It wanted a simple first SLO that captured a major user need and could be clearly explained and measured.
What core user promise did Evernote's first SLO focus on?	That users could access and sync their content across clients.
Why did Evernote choose third-party probing instead of only internal monitoring?	It wanted SLO measurement independent from its own stack so outages in shared infrastructure would still be visible.
How did Evernote define a service as down for SLO purposes?	A failed probe had to be confirmed by a second geographically separate probe before counting as downtime.
Why did Evernote count maintenance windows as downtime in SLO calculations?	Most users would experience planned maintenance as ordinary unavailability whether or not it was announced.
How did Evernote use SLOs operationally after defining them?	It reviewed SLO performance monthly with Google and used misses to prioritize fixes and improvements.
Why did Evernote accept imperfect first SLOs?	It preferred starting with a good-enough proxy and improving over time rather than waiting for a perfect but delayed solution.
What organizational effect did SLOs have at Evernote beyond measurement?	They improved the relationship between operations and development by giving both teams a common measure of service quality.
How did SLOs help Evernote make release decisions during a compressed timeline?	They quantified customer impact, which reduced subjective debate and helped narrow release windows.
Why did Evernote not want a hard reliability wall between itself and Google Cloud?	It wanted Google to understand which service characteristics mattered to Evernote users and to share responsibility for those outcomes.
How did shared SLOs change Evernote's relationship with Google?	Both sides used the same dashboards and could discuss incidents in terms of concrete impact on Evernote's customer-facing SLOs.
Why are cloud-provider global SLOs sometimes insufficient for a customer like Evernote?	A customer's local or regional outages can be diluted inside a much larger provider-wide aggregate metric.
How did shared SLOs improve major-incident handling for Evernote and Google?	Both parties could escalate quickly around the same measured customer impact instead of arguing from different views of severity.
What was Evernote's next planned evolution after its initial uptime SLO?	Move toward more detailed API and client-view measurements to better reflect actual user quality of service.
Why did The Home Depot need a common SLO language during its move to microservices and full-stack ownership?	Many small teams and services needed a shared way to express reliability expectations across dependencies.
What problem did SLOs solve for service-to-service communication at The Home Depot?	They let teams ask consistent questions about availability, latency, capacity, overload behavior, and historical performance.
What cultural problem existed at The Home Depot before its SLO effort?	Metrics were inconsistent and scattered, dependencies were opaque, and teams lacked a common way to discuss service reliability.
What four areas did The Home Depot's SLO culture project target?	Common vernacular, evangelism, automation, and incentives.
Why was establishing a common vernacular important at The Home Depot?	It gave teams a shared definition of what to measure and how to talk about reliability.
What did The Home Depot decide every microservice should publish?	Availability and latency SLOs for its APIs.
Why did The Home Depot decide not to set utilization SLOs?	Users care about responsiveness and capacity outcomes, not infrastructure utilization by itself.
Why did The Home Depot include traffic volume in its SLO framework?	It needed a standard way to express how much demand a service was expected to handle, especially during peaks.
Why did The Home Depot prefer latency percentiles over averages?	Percentiles better capture upper-bound user experience and tail behavior than arithmetic means.
Why did The Home Depot track both 4xx and 5xx errors but use only 5xx for SLOs?	It wanted visibility into all errors, but treated server-side failures as the reliability signal for objectives.
Why did The Home Depot keep tickets in its VALET framework?	Tickets were already an important operational signal and helped represent the amount of manual intervention a service still required.
Why was VALET useful beyond being a metric list?	It gave the company a memorable shared framework for evangelizing SLO concepts.
Why did The Home Depot invest heavily in evangelizing SLOs internally?	Teams unfamiliar with SLOs needed both education and cultural reinforcement before they would adopt them widely.
Why did The Home Depot build TPS Reports?	To automate collection and reporting of VALET metrics for services running in production.
What did the VALET service and dashboard add beyond raw metric collection?	They let teams register services, define SLO objectives, trend performance over time, and review services that missed targets.
How did regular VALET reviews help development teams?	They turned SLO trends into action items for improving reliability or revising unrealistic targets.
Why was building automation worth the effort at The Home Depot?	It helped the company scale from no SLO-supported services to hundreds in a relatively short time.
How can VALET be adapted for batch applications?	Volume becomes records processed, availability becomes whether jobs finish on time, latency becomes runtime, errors become failed records, and tickets become manual fixes.
How did VALET support destructive testing at The Home Depot?	It gave teams a way to measure the reliability impact of chaos-style tests in staging environments.
What future step did The Home Depot want after collecting many SLOs?	Build an error-budget culture that would affect release decisions when services drifted out of SLO.
Why did The Home Depot want more granular endpoint and consumer-specific SLOs?	Service-wide metrics can hide important differences between operations or between different callers of the same service.
