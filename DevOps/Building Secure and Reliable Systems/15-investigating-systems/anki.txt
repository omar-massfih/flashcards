Why should debugging be treated as a learnable investigation skill rather than as guesswork?	Systematic collection of data, hypotheses, and experiments makes progress possible even in unfamiliar systems.
Why does debugging require knowing how the system is supposed to work?	You can recognize abnormal behavior only by comparing what you observe against a mental model of intended behavior.
Why do large, mature systems produce more "zebra" bugs than small, new systems?	Common failures get eliminated over time, so rare interactions and scale effects become a larger share of what remains.
Why can rare failures become routine at large scale?	Very unlikely events on one machine become everyday occurrences when multiplied across huge fleets.
Why are checksums useful beyond hardware faults?	They can detect corruption from both hardware glitches and software bugs, provided their scope matches the failure you care about.
Why must the scope of a checksum match the system property you want to protect?	A checksum only detects corruption in the data it covers, so omitted context can let the wrong state still look valid.
Why should investigators write down both observations and expectations?	Bugs often live in the gap between what the system did and what people assumed it would do.
Why is normalized deviance dangerous for debugging and security?	Teams can get used to broken or risky behavior and stop noticing signals that should trigger deeper investigation.
Why should you try to reproduce bugs outside production when possible?	Reproduction in a safe environment lets you experiment freely, involve more people, and add intrusive diagnostics without harming users.
Why is isolating a problem to the smallest reproducing scope so valuable?	Smaller scopes eliminate irrelevant components and make root causes easier to reason about and test.
Why must investigators be cautious about confusing correlation with causation?	Events that happen together may share a symptom or a timeline without sharing a root cause.
Why should teams invest in observability when stuck debugging?	Better signals, traces, and internal visibility can turn opaque failures into testable explanations.
Why can cleaning up code be a legitimate debugging tactic?	Simpler code, better warnings, and clearer error handling can expose bugs that were hidden by accidental complexity.
Why can deleting a legacy system be safer than endlessly debugging it?	Removing unused or poorly understood code eliminates both bug surface and attack surface instead of preserving them forever.
Why should systems fail as close as possible to the source of the problem?	Early, local failure prevents downstream effects that obscure the root cause and spread damage.
Why is collaborative debugging valuable for both learning and incident response?	It exposes investigators to each other's tools and reasoning while making the team more practiced at solving problems together.
Why should suspected security compromises be escalated to specialists early?	Security investigations introduce attacker behavior, legal concerns, and evidence-handling risks that ordinary debugging does not.
Why can ordinary debugging actions be harmful during a security investigation?	Changing files or behavior may destroy evidence or tip off an attacker who is still active in the system.
Why do missing, corrupted, or strangely absent logs raise suspicion during an investigation?	Attackers often tamper with exactly the records investigators would rely on to reconstruct events.
Why should logging systems be designed to be append-only and hard to alter?	Attackers commonly try to erase their traces, so investigations depend on logs being difficult to rewrite after the fact.
Why should debugging logs be protected like other sensitive data?	Logs often contain metadata or contents that reveal user behavior, secrets, or internal system structure.
Why should investigators log security-relevant actions even when they seem operationally routine?	Actions on data, ownership, and account state often become the key timeline for later reconstruction.
Why should logging systems degrade gracefully instead of simply stopping at capacity limits?	Summarized or lower-fidelity retention is far more useful during incidents than losing all historical signal.
Why is robust emergency debugging access necessary even when strong access controls exist?	When the normal security systems fail, responders still need a controlled way to investigate and repair them.
