What is architectural thinking?	Viewing systems from an architectural point of view by considering structure, interactions, trade-offs, and long-term consequences.
What is the difference between software architecture and software design?	Architecture is primarily about system structure, while design is primarily about the appearance and localized implementation details within that structure.
What three criteria help place a decision on the spectrum between architecture and design?	Whether it is strategic or tactical, how much effort it takes to change, and how significant the trade-offs are.
Why are strategic decisions usually more architectural than tactical decisions?	Because they are longer-term, involve more planning and stakeholders, and shape the system more broadly.

What is modularity in software architecture?	The logical grouping of related code into cohesive units such as packages, namespaces, classes, functions, or components.
Why is modularity considered an implicit architectural characteristic?	Sustainable systems need modular distinction and orderly communication even when requirements never state that explicitly.
What is the difference between modularity and granularity?	Modularity is about breaking a system into parts; granularity is about how large or small those parts are.

Why should architects care about how developers package code?	Packaging affects reuse, coupling, analysis, and how hard the system is to restructure later.

What is cohesion?	The degree to which the parts of a module belong together.
What is functional cohesion?	A module where all parts contribute directly to one well-defined responsibility.
What is sequential cohesion?	A relationship where one part's output becomes another part's input in sequence.
What is communicational cohesion?	A grouping where parts operate on the same data or contribute to the same output.
What is procedural cohesion?	A grouping where parts mainly belong together because they must run in a specific order.
What is temporal cohesion?	A grouping based mainly on things that must happen at the same time, such as startup tasks.
What is logical cohesion?	A grouping of operations that are conceptually related but not strongly functionally related.
What is coincidental cohesion?	A module whose contents are unrelated except for being placed together.
What does a high LCOM score indicate?	A lack of cohesion in methods, suggesting a class may really contain multiple unrelated responsibilities.
What is afferent coupling?	The number of incoming dependencies on a code artifact.
What is efferent coupling?	The number of outgoing dependencies from a code artifact to others.
Why does high efferent coupling often increase instability?	Because the artifact depends on many others and is more likely to break when they change.
What does abstractness measure?	The ratio of abstract artifacts to the total of abstract plus concrete artifacts in a codebase or module.
What does instability measure?	The ratio of outgoing coupling to total incoming plus outgoing coupling.
What does Distance from the Main Sequence measure?	How far a module is from the ideal balance between abstractness and instability.
What does the Zone of Uselessness represent?	Code that is too abstract and therefore hard to use productively.
What does the Zone of Pain represent?	Code that is too concrete and rigid, making it brittle and hard to maintain.
Why do architecture metrics still require interpretation?	Because they expose structural patterns but cannot fully distinguish good complexity from bad complexity or explain intent.
What is connascence?	A way of describing coupling where changing one element requires another to change to preserve system correctness.
What is the difference between static and dynamic connascence?	Static connascence appears in source structure, while dynamic connascence depends on runtime behavior or ordering.
What is Connascence of Name?	Multiple elements must agree on the same identifier or name.
What is Connascence of Type?	Multiple elements must agree on the same type.
What is Connascence of Meaning?	Multiple elements must agree on the meaning of a value or convention, such as a magic number or string.
What is Connascence of Position?	Multiple elements must agree on the ordering of values, such as positional parameters.
What is Connascence of Algorithm?	Multiple elements must agree on the same algorithm in order to work correctly together.
What is Connascence of Timing?	Correctness depends on elements happening at the right time relative to one another.
What is Connascence of Values?	Several values must change together to preserve correctness.
What is Connascence of Identity?	Multiple elements must reference the same specific entity.
Why is static connascence generally preferable to dynamic connascence?	Static forms are easier to analyze and refactor, while dynamic forms are usually more fragile and harder to reason about.
What does the strength of connascence describe?	How hard a form of coupling is to change or refactor.
What does the locality of connascence describe?	How close the coupled elements are to one another in the codebase or architecture.
What does the degree of connascence describe?	How many elements are affected by the coupling.
What is the Rule of Degree for connascence?	Refactor stronger forms of connascence into weaker forms when possible.
What is the Rule of Locality for connascence?	As distance between elements increases, prefer weaker forms of connascence.
What are the three general modularity guidelines based on connascence?	Minimize overall connascence; minimize connascence across boundaries; maximize connascence within boundaries.
Why is connascence useful to architects?	It gives more precise language for identifying, discussing, and refactoring different kinds of coupling.

What does high functional cohesion mean in the context of architecture quanta?	The quantum performs a unified, purposeful business function rather than mixing unrelated responsibilities.

What is component-based thinking in software architecture?	Seeing a system as a set of logical components that interact to perform business functions.
Why should architects create a logical architecture before jumping to a physical architecture?	Logical architecture clarifies where functionality lives and how responsibilities fit together before physical deployment choices are made.

Why should component identification be iterative rather than “perfect” up front?	Architects learn more as requirements evolve, so components should be refined through continuous feedback and restructuring.

What is the Actor/Action approach to identifying initial components?	Derive candidate components from the major actions performed by each actor, including the system itself.
What is the Entity Trap in component design?	Creating components from domain entities, such as `Order Manager`, instead of from clear functional responsibilities.

How can architectural characteristics affect component granularity?	Qualities such as scalability, reliability, elasticity, and agility may justify splitting or combining components beyond purely functional boundaries.
Why might one functionally unified component be split because of architectural characteristics?	Different parts of the same workflow may need different levels of scalability, availability, or reliability.
What is static coupling between components?	Direct synchronous dependency where components communicate or depend on one another structurally.
What is temporal coupling between components?	A dependency where correctness relies on actions happening in a particular order or timing relationship.
Why is temporal coupling harder to detect than static coupling?	It usually emerges from runtime sequencing or transactions rather than obvious structural dependencies in code.
What does the Law of Demeter state in software architecture?	A component or service should have limited knowledge of other components or services.
How does the Law of Demeter reduce coupling?	By deferring knowledge of downstream actions to nearer components instead of making one component know too much about the rest of the system.
What important trade-off accompanies applying the Law of Demeter?	It often redistributes coupling to different components rather than reducing total system-wide coupling.
What is a Big Ball of Mud?	An architecture with no clear structure, widespread coupling, and uncontrolled growth.
Why is a Big Ball of Mud so dangerous?	Because changes have unpredictable ripple effects, making the system hard to maintain, test, deploy, and evolve.
What is top-level partitioning in software architecture?	The primary way an architecture organizes its highest-level components.
What is technical partitioning in architecture?	Organizing top-level components by technical capabilities such as presentation, business logic, or persistence.
What is domain partitioning in architecture?	Organizing top-level components around business domains or workflows rather than technical layers.
What is a major advantage of technical partitioning?	It clearly separates technical concerns and makes certain categories of code easy to find.
What is a major disadvantage of technical partitioning?	Business workflows get smeared across multiple layers, increasing global coupling around domain changes.
What is a major advantage of domain partitioning?	It aligns the codebase more closely with how the business actually works and changes.
Why has industry interest shifted toward domain partitioning?	It better supports domain-driven design, cross-functional teams, and migration toward modular monoliths and microservices.
What does Conway's Law say?	System designs tend to mirror the communication structures of the organizations that build them.
What is the Inverse Conway Maneuver?	Deliberately shaping team structures to encourage the architecture you want.
What is the key distinction between monolithic and distributed architectures?	Monolithic styles deploy as a single unit, while distributed styles consist of multiple deployment units communicating remotely.
Why do distributed architectures share a distinct set of trade-offs?	Remote communication introduces network-related failure modes and operational complexity that monoliths largely avoid.
What does the fallacy "the network is reliable" warn against?	Distributed systems must assume network failures will happen even when services themselves are healthy.
What does the fallacy "latency is zero" warn against?	Remote calls are always slower than local calls, and tail latency can make fine-grained distributed designs impractical.
What does the fallacy "bandwidth is infinite" warn against?	Distributed communication consumes real network capacity, and excessive payloads or chatty interactions can seriously hurt performance.
What does the fallacy "the network is secure" warn against?	Every distributed endpoint increases the attack surface and must be explicitly secured.
What does the fallacy "the topology never changes" warn against?	Network infrastructure changes regularly, and those changes can invalidate architectural assumptions.
What does the fallacy "there is only one administrator" warn against?	Distributed systems depend on coordination across many operational owners, not one person with full control.
What does the fallacy "transport cost is zero" warn against?	Distributed architectures require additional infrastructure and therefore real monetary cost.
What does the fallacy "the network is homogeneous" warn against?	Real networks mix vendors and environments, so interoperability and behavior are not perfectly uniform.
What does the fallacy "versioning is easy" warn against?	Supporting evolving service contracts creates difficult trade-offs about scope, compatibility, and deprecation.
Why should architects favor simpler designs when possible?	Architectural decisions often outlive the assumptions that motivated them, so simpler designs age better.
What is the layered architecture style?	An architecture style that organizes components into horizontal layers, each with a specific technical responsibility.
Why is layered architecture so common in legacy systems?	It is simple, familiar, and relatively inexpensive to build and maintain.
Why is layered architecture considered a technically partitioned style?	Top-level components are organized by technical role rather than by business domain.
Why does layered architecture fit poorly with domain-driven design?	A single business domain gets spread across multiple technical layers, making domain changes harder.
What is a closed layer in layered architecture?	A layer that requests cannot skip on the way to lower layers.
What is an open layer in layered architecture?	A layer that requests may bypass to reach a lower layer directly.
What are layers of isolation in layered architecture?	The idea that changes in one layer should not affect others as long as the layer contracts stay the same.
What trade-off comes with using open layers?	They can reduce indirection for some requests, but they increase coupling and make the system more brittle.
Why might architects add a Services layer to a layered architecture?	To isolate shared objects or utilities and prevent upper layers from accessing them directly through the Business layer.
Why must open and closed layer rules be documented clearly?	Without clear access rules, developers create tightly coupled paths that are hard to test, maintain, and deploy.
What is the Architecture Sinkhole antipattern?	A situation where requests pass through multiple layers with little or no business logic added along the way.
Why is the Architecture Sinkhole antipattern harmful?	It adds unnecessary processing and object creation, hurting performance and wasting resources.
How can the 80-20 rule help identify whether the sinkhole antipattern is a serious problem?	If most requests simply pass through layers without meaningful work, layered architecture is probably a poor fit.
Why are cloud deployment options limited for layered architecture?	Because the style is usually monolithic and layered workflows often traverse most of the stack, making separated deployments latency-sensitive.
Why does layered architecture have weak fault tolerance?	A failure in one part of the monolith can crash the entire deployment unit.
Why does layered architecture have poor deployability?	Even small changes usually require redeploying the entire unit, increasing coordination cost and deployment risk.
Why do scalability and elasticity rate poorly in layered architecture?	The monolithic architecture quantum forces the system to scale largely as a whole rather than by independently scaling parts.
When is layered architecture a good choice?	For small, simple applications or early-stage systems with tight budget and time constraints.
When should layered architecture be avoided?	When the system is large enough that maintainability, agility, testability, and deployability start to degrade badly.
Why is governance relatively strong for layered architecture?	Common structural testing tools and fitness functions can enforce layer boundaries and access rules effectively.
What is the modular monolith architecture style?	A monolithic architecture style that groups functionality by business domain into modules within a single deployment unit.
Why is modular monolith considered a domain-partitioned architecture?	Because its top-level organization is based on business domains or subdomains rather than technical layers.
What trade-off comes with the monolithic structure option in modular monolith architecture?	It is simpler to maintain and deploy, but it requires strict governance to prevent excessive reuse and blurry module boundaries.
When does the modular structure option work best in modular monolith architecture?	When modules are relatively independent or require distinct expertise, making cleaner boundaries worth the extra structure.
Why can the modular structure option lose effectiveness when modules depend heavily on one another?	Frequent intermodule communication creates compile-time dependencies and dependency-management pain, such as JAR or DLL hell.
What is the peer-to-peer communication approach in modular monolith architecture?	One module directly invokes classes or methods in another module.
What is the mediator approach in modular monolith architecture?	A mediator component accepts requests and routes them to the appropriate modules, reducing direct module-to-module dependencies.
What trade-off comes with using a mediator in modular monolith architecture?	Modules become less coupled to one another, but they are all coupled to the mediator.
What data topology is most common in modular monolith architecture?	A single shared database, though independent modules may also have their own databases when that better fits their context.
What warning signs suggest a modular monolith is getting too big?	Changes take too long, unrelated areas break unexpectedly, teams interfere with one another, or startup time becomes excessive.
What is a primary risk of excessive code reuse in modular monolith architecture?	It blurs module boundaries and can turn the system into an unstructured monolith.
What does too much intermodule communication usually indicate in modular monolith architecture?	The domain boundaries were probably defined poorly and need to be reconsidered.
Why do domain-aligned cross-functional teams fit modular monolith architecture well?	They can own a module end to end, from interface through data, with less cross-team coordination.
Why do technically organized teams fit modular monolith architecture poorly?	Domain changes cut across every technical team, increasing communication overhead and slowing delivery.
What are the main strengths of modular monolith architecture?	Low cost, conceptual simplicity, and good modularity within a monolithic deployment.
Why are deployability and testability only modestly better in modular monolith than in layered architecture?	Modules improve separation of concerns, but the system is still deployed and tested largely as one monolith.
Why does modular monolith architecture rate poorly for scalability and elasticity?	The monolithic deployment forces most scaling decisions to happen at the whole-system level rather than per module.
Why does modular monolith architecture have weak fault tolerance and availability?	A failure such as an out-of-memory condition can crash the entire deployment unit, and monolith startup times increase recovery time.
When is modular monolith architecture a good choice?	When budgets or timelines are tight, teams are domain-focused, or a new system needs a simpler starting point with room to evolve later.
When should modular monolith architecture be avoided?	When the system needs strong operational characteristics such as high scalability, elasticity, availability, fault tolerance, or frequent technology-layer changes.
What is the pipeline architecture style?	An architecture style that processes data through a sequence of self-contained filters connected by one-way pipes.
What are the two component types in pipeline architecture?	Filters, which perform the work, and pipes, which pass data from one filter to the next.
Why should filters in pipeline architecture usually be stateless and single-purpose?	Small focused filters are easier to compose, replace, test, and reason about than filters that accumulate multiple responsibilities.
Why does pipeline architecture encourage compositional reuse?	Simple one-way filters can be recombined into many useful workflows without tightly coupling them.
How can pipeline architecture be deployed as a distributed architecture?	Each filter, or group of filters, can be deployed as a separate service with synchronous or asynchronous remote communication between them.
Why can pipeline architecture use either a single database or multiple databases?	Although the style is often monolithic, different filters may own or read different data stores depending on the workflow.
What is a common risk of overloading filters in pipeline architecture?	The architecture loses its single-purpose modularity and starts collapsing into an unstructured monolith.
What does bidirectional communication between filters usually indicate in pipeline architecture?	Either the style is a poor fit or the filters have been defined with the wrong boundaries.
Why is error handling difficult in pipeline architecture?	Once the pipeline is in motion, it can be hard to decide how to stop, unwind, or recover from failures cleanly.
Why do contracts between filters require strict governance in pipeline architecture?	Changes to the data passed through a pipe can break downstream filters that depend on that contract.
What are the main strengths of pipeline architecture?	Low cost, conceptual simplicity, and good modularity through narrowly scoped filters.
Why are deployability and testability only moderate in pipeline architecture?	Filters improve modularity, but the style is still commonly deployed and tested as a monolith.
Why does pipeline architecture usually score poorly on scalability and elasticity?	Most implementations are monolithic, so the whole deployment unit tends to scale together rather than by filter.
Why does pipeline architecture usually have weak fault tolerance and availability?	A failure in one part of the monolith can crash the whole deployment unit, and recovery is slowed by monolith startup time.
What trade-off comes with distributing a pipeline asynchronously?	It can improve scalability, elasticity, and fault tolerance, but it increases cost and architectural complexity.
When is pipeline architecture a good choice?	When the system has distinct, ordered, deterministic, one-way processing steps and needs a simple workflow-oriented structure.
When should pipeline architecture be avoided?	When the workflow requires back-and-forth communication, is highly nondeterministic, or needs strong monolithic scalability and fault-tolerance characteristics.
What is the microkernel architecture style?	A monolithic architecture style that splits functionality between a minimal core system and independent plug-in components.
When is microkernel architecture a natural fit?	When a system needs product-style extensibility or domain-specific customization without constantly changing the whole core.
What is the core system in microkernel architecture?	The minimal stable functionality needed to run the system, often representing the application's happy path.
Why does microkernel architecture move cyclomatic complexity out of the core system?	Isolating specialized logic in plug-ins improves extensibility, maintainability, and testability.
What are plug-in components in microkernel architecture?	Standalone components that add specialized processing, features, or customization around the core system.
Why should plug-in components ideally avoid dependencies on one another?	Plug-in-to-plug-in dependencies create transitive version conflicts and make the architecture much harder to manage.
What is the trade-off between compile-based and runtime-based plug-ins?	Compile-based plug-ins are simpler to manage, while runtime plug-ins allow addition or removal without redeploying the whole application.
What benefits come from remote plug-in access in microkernel architecture?	It can improve decoupling, throughput, runtime changeability, and asynchronous responsiveness.
What trade-off comes with implementing microkernel plug-ins as remote services?	The style becomes distributed and more complex and costly, while requests still depend on the monolithic core system.
What is a plug-in registry in microkernel architecture?	A structure the core system uses to discover which plug-ins exist and how to invoke them.
Why are standard contracts important between the core system and plug-ins?	They let the core interact with plug-ins consistently without specialized logic for each one.
Why do microkernel plug-ins usually avoid direct access to the shared central database?	Keeping database access in the core system reduces coupling so schema changes affect fewer components.
Why might a plug-in component own its own data store?	To keep plug-in-specific rules or state self-contained and isolated from the rest of the system.
Why is a volatile core a major risk in microkernel architecture?	If the core changes constantly, the architecture loses the stability and isolation that justify using plug-ins in the first place.
Why are plug-in dependencies a major risk in microkernel architecture?	The core must resolve conflicting transitive dependencies, which adds significant operational and design complexity.
Why are testability and deployability above average in microkernel architecture?	Changes can often be isolated to self-contained plug-ins, reducing the scope and risk of testing and deployment.
Why do scalability, elasticity, and fault tolerance rate poorly in microkernel architecture?	The architecture is typically monolithic, and all requests still pass through the core system.
When is microkernel architecture a good choice?	When the system needs extensibility, user customization, or localized business-rule variation around a stable core.
What is the service-based architecture style?	A distributed architecture style built from separately deployed, coarse-grained domain services, usually paired with a UI layer and often a shared database.
Why is service-based architecture considered a pragmatic distributed style?	It delivers modularity and independent deployment with much less complexity and cost than finer-grained distributed styles such as microservices.
What are domain services in service-based architecture?	Coarse-grained services aligned to a business domain or subdomain, such as ordering, shipping, or invoicing.
Why are domain services usually coarse-grained in service-based architecture?	Coarser service boundaries keep related business logic and data changes together, reducing coordination overhead between services.
How is request orchestration usually handled in service-based architecture?	A domain service's API facade usually orchestrates the internal business steps needed to fulfill the request.
How does request orchestration in service-based architecture differ from microservices?	Service-based architecture usually orchestrates work inside a coarse-grained service, while microservices often coordinate multiple remote fine-grained services.
Why can service-based architecture usually rely on ACID transactions?	Because related work often stays inside one coarse-grained domain service, allowing normal database commits and rollbacks within that service boundary.
What transaction trade-off separates service-based architecture from microservices?	Service-based architecture favors local ACID transactions inside a domain service, while microservices often need eventual consistency and compensating actions across services.
What role can an API Gateway play in service-based architecture?	It can centralize cross-cutting concerns such as security, metrics, auditing, service discovery, and load balancing.
What is a major risk of using a shared monolithic database in service-based architecture?	Schema changes can ripple across many services, making changes costly, risky, and coordination-heavy.
Why is a single shared entity library an antipattern in service-based architecture?	A schema change can force rebuilding and redeploying every service, even those that do not actually use the changed tables.
How can logical database partitioning reduce change impact in service-based architecture?	Partitioning the schema by domain and matching it with separate shared libraries limits retesting and redeployment to affected services.
Why should interservice communication usually be minimized in service-based architecture?	The style works best when domains are largely independent and most coordination happens inside services or at the UI or gateway layer.
What does excessive interservice communication usually indicate in service-based architecture?	That the domain boundaries are poorly partitioned or that a different architecture style may be a better fit.
Why should service-based architecture usually avoid having too many domain services?	Too many services increase testing, deployment, monitoring, and database-change complexity; a practical upper limit is about 12.
Why do technically partitioned teams fit poorly with service-based architecture?	Requirements usually cut across UI, business, and data work inside one domain, forcing heavy interteam coordination in technically siloed organizations.
Why do scalability and elasticity score lower in service-based architecture than in microservices?	Scaling a coarse-grained service replicates more functionality than scaling a smaller single-purpose service, making it less efficient and less cost-effective.
Why is service-based architecture a natural fit for domain-driven design?	Each coarse-grained service can map cleanly to a domain boundary, making domain logic easier to isolate, change, and deploy.
Why can service-based architecture be a good stepping stone toward microservices?	It lets teams first find stable domain boundaries, then split only the domains that truly need finer-grained services.
When is service-based architecture a good choice?	When a system needs domain modularity, independent deployment, and solid fault isolation without the full complexity of highly distributed fine-grained architectures.
What is the event-driven architecture style?	A distributed asynchronous architecture style built from decoupled components that trigger and react to events.
How does an event-based model differ from a request-based model?	A request-based model synchronously asks the system to do specific work, while an event-based model reacts to something that has already happened.
What is a derived event in event-driven architecture?	A new event an event processor publishes after completing some processing in response to an earlier event.
Why are derived events essential in event-driven architecture?	They let downstream processing continue asynchronously without tightly coupling processors to one another.
What is the difference between an event and a message in architecture?	An event announces something that already happened, while a message is usually a command or request for something to be done.
Why are events usually more decoupled than messages?	Events are typically broadcast without requiring a direct response, while messages are usually directed to a specific receiver and often expect one.
What is an extensible derived event?	A derived event that may currently have no consumers but still provides a hook for adding future behavior without changing existing processors.
Why can asynchronous communication improve responsiveness without improving end-to-end performance?	It can acknowledge acceptance of work quickly even when the full processing still takes a long time in the background.
What is a major trade-off of asynchronous communication in event-driven architecture?	It improves responsiveness and decoupling but makes error handling and user feedback more difficult.
Why are broadcast capabilities important in event-driven architecture?	They let one processor publish an event without knowing which other processors will react or what actions they will take.
What is the main trade-off between data-based and key-based event payloads?	Data-based payloads favor performance and fewer lookups, while key-based payloads favor simpler contracts, less bandwidth, and lower static coupling.
Why can data-based event payloads create stamp coupling?	Many consumers share the same large payload structure even though each one only uses part of it.
What is an anemic event in event-driven architecture?	An event whose payload lacks enough context for downstream processors to make decisions or complete useful work.
What is the Swarm of Gnats antipattern in event-driven architecture?	Triggering too many overly fine-grained derived events from one action, overwhelming the system and obscuring the overall event flow.
How can architects avoid the Swarm of Gnats antipattern?	Model derived events around meaningful outcomes or state changes rather than emitting many tiny low-level events.
What is a poison event in event-driven architecture?	An event that keeps triggering a looping chain of responses indefinitely between processors.
What is request-reply processing in event-driven architecture?	A pseudo-synchronous messaging pattern where a requester sends work asynchronously and later waits for a correlated reply.
Why is the correlation-ID technique usually preferred over temporary reply queues in request-reply processing?	It avoids the overhead of creating and deleting a dedicated temporary queue for every request.
What is the choreographed event-driven topology?	A topology where processors react to events directly and no central component controls the workflow.
What is the mediator topology in event-driven architecture?	An orchestrated topology where an event mediator controls workflow steps and coordinates processors through directed messages.
What is the main trade-off between choreographed and mediator event-driven topologies?	Choreography favors scalability and decoupling, while mediation favors workflow control, state tracking, and error handling.
What is the main database-topology trade-off in event-driven architecture?	More isolated databases improve fault isolation, scalability, and change control, but often increase synchronous data-fetching between processors.
Why is too much synchronous communication a warning sign in event-driven architecture?	It undermines the style's asynchronous decoupling and suggests either poor boundaries or a poor architectural fit.
Why can event-driven architecture be difficult to test and debug?	Its asynchronous, parallel, and nondeterministic event flows make end-to-end behavior hard to predict and reproduce.
Why is event-driven architecture considered highly evolutionary?	New behavior can often be added by introducing new processors that subscribe to existing events instead of changing current ones.
When is event-driven architecture a good choice?	When a system must react to events with high responsiveness, scalability, extensibility, and tolerance for asynchronous eventual consistency.
What is the space-based architecture style designed to solve?	Extreme scalability, elasticity, and concurrency problems caused by central database bottlenecks in high-load systems.
How does space-based architecture remove the database from the critical request path?	It keeps transactional data in replicated in-memory grids inside processing units and writes to the database asynchronously.
What is a processing unit in space-based architecture?	A deployable unit containing application logic plus the in-memory data grid and replication capability needed to process requests.
What is virtualized middleware in space-based architecture?	The collection of infrastructure components that route requests, coordinate data replication, manage orchestration, and scale processing units up or down.
What is the role of the data grid in space-based architecture?	It keeps same-named in-memory caches synchronized across processing-unit instances.
Why is the data grid the most critical part of space-based architecture?	It replaces direct database dependency during normal processing by synchronizing the in-memory state that all units rely on.
What is replicated caching in space-based architecture?	A model where each processing unit keeps its own in-memory copy of the cache and updates are replicated to the others.
What is distributed caching in space-based architecture?	A model where processing units access a centralized external cache rather than storing full cache state locally.
What is the main trade-off between replicated and distributed caching?	Replicated caching favors performance and fault tolerance, while distributed caching favors stronger consistency and support for larger or more dynamic datasets.
When is replicated caching usually the better choice in space-based architecture?	When the cache is relatively small, data is fairly static, update rates are modest, and performance or fault tolerance matters most.
When is distributed caching usually the better choice in space-based architecture?	When cache volumes are large, update rates are high, or consistency requirements are stronger than raw speed.
Why is a near-cache usually not recommended in space-based architecture?	It introduces inconsistent front-cache contents across processing units, which makes performance and behavior less predictable.
What is a data pump in space-based architecture?	An asynchronous mechanism for sending cache updates from processing units to downstream database-writing components.
Why are data pumps used in space-based architecture?	They preserve asynchronous processing and eventual consistency without forcing processing units to write directly to the database.
Why does space-based architecture usually provide only eventual consistency with the database?	Cache updates are propagated to the database asynchronously through data pumps and data writers rather than committed inline with the request.
What is a data collision in space-based architecture?	A conflict where the same cached data is updated in multiple active replicas before replication completes, leaving caches inconsistent.
What factors increase the likelihood of data collisions in replicated caches?	More active instances sharing the cache, higher update rates, smaller cache sizes, and higher replication latency.
Why is space-based architecture hard to test?	Its main strengths appear only under very high load, and simulating that scale and elasticity is expensive and operationally risky.
When is space-based architecture a good choice?	When a system needs extreme responsiveness, scalability, and elasticity under highly variable or bursty concurrent load.
What is orchestration-driven service-oriented architecture (SOA)?	A distributed architecture style organized around a strict service taxonomy and a central orchestration engine that coordinates workflows across service layers.
What is the core organizing idea behind orchestration-driven SOA?	Separate services into technical layers with distinct responsibilities and maximize reuse through shared enterprise services.
What is the role of the orchestration engine in orchestration-driven SOA?	It coordinates workflows, message transformation, service interaction, and often transaction boundaries across the architecture.
Why does the orchestration engine become a major coupling point in orchestration-driven SOA?	Because nearly all message flow and workflow coordination pass through it, making the rest of the architecture depend on its behavior and constraints.
Why is orchestration-driven SOA considered extremely technically partitioned?	A single business change is often spread across many technical layers and teams rather than being contained within one domain-aligned unit.
Why does reuse create coupling in orchestration-driven SOA?	Shared reusable services become dependencies for many consumers, so changes ripple widely across the system.
Why can canonical services become a problem in orchestration-driven SOA?	They force many consumers to share one model, increasing complexity and causing unrelated teams to absorb changes they do not care about.
Why did orchestration-driven SOA make common changes expensive?	Even small feature changes could require coordinated updates across several layers, services, teams, and sometimes transaction definitions.
Why were declarative distributed transactions in orchestration-driven SOA difficult in practice?	Real workflows and failure modes made transaction boundaries hard to abstract cleanly, leading to complexity, edge cases, and brittle behavior.
Why does orchestration-driven SOA usually form a single architectural quantum despite being distributed?	The shared database patterns and especially the central orchestration engine create broad synchronous and behavioral coupling across the system.
Why do deployability and testability score poorly in orchestration-driven SOA?	Changes are highly coupled across layers, and testing or deploying one part in isolation is difficult because so much behavior depends on the whole workflow.
What modern architectural lesson came from the failure of orchestration-driven SOA?	Extreme technical partitioning and aggressive reuse can hurt agility, maintainability, and team effectiveness more than they help.
What is the Accidental SOA antipattern?	Gradually letting an integration tool such as an ESB grow into the central orchestrator and coupling point for the whole architecture.
How can architects use ESBs safely today without rebuilding orchestration-driven SOA?	Keep them narrowly focused on integration concerns and enforce clear boundaries so orchestration and coupling do not spread across the whole system.
When is orchestration-driven SOA still useful today?	Primarily in integration-heavy environments where legacy systems, packages, and newer services must participate in coordinated workflows.
What is the core idea behind microservices architecture?	Build an application as a set of independently deployable, domain-aligned services that each own the functionality and data needed for their bounded context.
Why is microservices sometimes called a "share nothing" architecture?	Because each bounded context avoids coupling to code, schemas, and databases outside its own service boundary.
How does domain-driven design influence microservices?	Microservices physically implement bounded contexts so each service aligns with a specific domain, subdomain, or workflow.
Why does microservices often prefer duplication over reuse?	Reusing code or data across service boundaries increases coupling, while duplication preserves service independence.
What is the main performance trade-off of microservices?	Work that was once local becomes network communication, increasing latency, security overhead, and coordination cost.
What does good microservice granularity try to capture?	A cohesive domain capability or workflow boundary rather than an arbitrarily tiny technical unit.
Why is making microservices too small a common failure mode?	Overly fine-grained services need excessive communication and coordination, creating a distributed system with lots of overhead and little benefit.
How can transactions help reveal better service boundaries in microservices?	If multiple pieces must repeatedly participate in one transaction, they may belong in the same service boundary.
Why can heavy choreography between services suggest the wrong granularity?	If useful work requires many services to constantly call one another, the boundaries may be too fragmented and should be reconsidered.
Why is data isolation required in microservices?	A shared database or schema creates coupling that breaks bounded contexts and makes services hard to evolve independently.
What is the usual database pattern in microservices?	Database per service, where each service owns its own schema or database and exposes data to others only through contracts.
Why is a single shared database a poor fit for microservices?	It creates broad coupling around schema changes, scalability, availability, and deployment coordination, undermining service independence.
When can a few microservices share a database without abandoning bounded context entirely?	When they are really part of a broader bounded context that legitimately needs shared data and coordinated change.
What should an API Gateway do in microservices?	Handle routing and cross-cutting concerns such as security, logging, and monitoring without containing business logic.
Why should the API layer avoid business orchestration in microservices?	Business logic in the gateway turns it into a mediator, weakening domain boundaries and reintroducing central coupling.
What is operational reuse in microservices?	Sharing operational capabilities like logging, monitoring, and circuit breaking without coupling business-domain code.
How does the Sidecar pattern support microservices?	It attaches shared operational components to each service so teams can standardize cross-cutting behavior without centralizing domain logic.
What is a service mesh in microservices?	An operational layer that connects service sidecars to provide consistent control over concerns like discovery, traffic management, and observability.
What is choreography in microservices?	A coordination style where services call or react to one another directly without a central coordinator.
What is orchestration in microservices?	A coordination style where a dedicated mediator service manages the workflow across multiple services.
What is the trade-off between choreography and orchestration in microservices?	Choreography preserves decoupling but can make coordination harder, while orchestration simplifies workflow control but introduces more coupling.
Why should architects usually avoid transactions across microservice boundaries?	They create strong runtime coupling and often indicate that the service boundaries are too fine-grained.
What is a saga in microservices?	A distributed transaction pattern that coordinates a sequence of local service operations and uses compensation if part of the workflow fails.
What is a compensating transaction in a saga?	An action that semantically undoes an earlier successful step when a later step in the saga fails.
What is the Grains of Sand antipattern in microservices?	Creating services so small that the system becomes dominated by interservice communication and coordination overhead.
Why is sharing custom libraries risky in microservices?	Shared code leaks functionality across bounded contexts, so changes in one place can break supposedly independent services.
What kind of team topology fits microservices best?	Domain-aligned cross-functional teams, because the architecture is partitioned around business domains rather than technical layers.
Why do microservices score highly on deployability and evolvability?	Small, independently deployable services let teams change and release parts of the system without coordinating the whole architecture.
Why does microservices often have strong fault tolerance?	Failures are more isolated because services are separately deployed and do not all share the same process or data boundary.
